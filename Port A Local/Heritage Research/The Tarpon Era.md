# The Tarpon Era — Complete Research File

> Research compiled April 2026 for Port A Local Island Stories content.

---

## THE BEGINNING

### When Tarpon Fishing Started
- Historian Dr. John Guthrie Ford believes the fishery was discovered soon after the Aransas Pass jetty project began in **1880**. Workers saw massive schools of tarpon and began hiring Mustang Island boatmen.
- First tarpon caught on rod and reel anywhere: **Florida, 1885**. Within a decade, Port Aransas had reoriented around the fish.
- Before 1876: no mention of tarpon or rod-and-reel fishing in the Mercer Logs (primary early island records).
- Pre-1880 island economy: cattle roundups, ship piloting, commercial netting, harvesting roseate spoonbills for plumage trade.

### The Name Changes
- **1855**: Robert Ainsworth Mercer built the first cabin on Mustang Island
- **1880**: William and Ed Mercer opened a store (November 1880)
- **1888**: First post office — called "Ropesville" (for Elihu Harrison Ropes)
- **July 17, 1896**: Renamed **"Tarpon"** per postmaster Emma A. Roberts. Population ~250.
- **December 23, 1910**: "Port Aransas" recorded
- **April 1, 1911**: "Port Aransas" officially adopted — aspirational, planning to become a major seaport
- **November 25, 1911**: Town incorporated

### Why This Spot
The Aransas Pass jetties (stone jetty 1888; granite jetties begun 1885, completed 1919) created the deep-water channel where tarpon congregated in immense schools. The jetties funneled baitfish and created predictable, fishable staging grounds right at the pass.

## THE FISHING CULTURE

### Tackle and Techniques (circa 1909)
- Rod: 5-1/2 feet in length
- Line: #9 linen line
- Bait: Live mullet, cast in front of approaching tarpon schools
- A drying spool for linen line was standard equipment (one displayed in museum)
- Tackle manufacturers like Pflueger brought equipment to test against tarpon
- A vintage 1920s reel from the area: "German silver" with wooden components, rust-free decades later

### A Typical Fishing Day (1920s-1940s)
- Anglers called at 5:00 AM
- Breakfast at the Tarpon Inn (which packed lunches and served dinner on return)
- On the fishing grounds by 6:00 AM
- Guides rowed clients through the pass to the open Gulf
- Trolled along the surf line where immense schools swam in deep guts between sandbars
- Multiple boats competed simultaneously for the same schools
- Eight-hour fishing day was standard
- Fish photographed rather than eaten — tarpon are not good to eat

### The Guides
- **Ed Cotter**: First boatman to operate a power fishing boat. ~1900, Col. Ned Green of New York purchased a powerboat and paid Cotter to go to Chicago to learn to operate its Packard engine (ran on naphtha). By ~1904, Cotter used the powerboat to tow skiffs — **believed to be the first time a motorized boat was used for sport fishing in the U.S.** Also owner of the Tarpon Inn. Cotter Avenue named for him.
- **Barney Farley Sr.**: Arrived ~1910. Organized the first Tarpon Rodeo in 1932 (became the Deep Sea Roundup). Guided FDR in 1937.
- **Fred Farley**: Barney's brother. Master craftsman from Birmingham, Alabama. Established the boat works in 1914.
- **Ted Mathews**: Pilot and guide on FDR's boat, May 8, 1937.
- **Don Farley**: Barney's nephew, captained boats for the presidential visit.
- **Florida Roberts**: Arrived 1929, example of skilled fishermen drawn to the thriving marketplace.

### Record Catches
- **1909 season**: 297 tarpon caught on light tackle. Only 12 exceeded 6 feet. One was a world record at 6'6".
- **1909**: One angler caught seven tarpon in a single day.
- **July 13, 1931**: Dr. Stirling E. Russ caught a 7-foot, 168-pound tarpon (mounted at the Tarpon Inn).
- **May 8, 1937**: FDR caught a 5-foot, 77-pound tarpon at 3:27 PM.
- **1932 first Tarpon Rodeo**: A tarpon caught by a woman is still mounted in the museum.
- **Texas state record**: 229 pounds, 90 inches (caught 2017).
- Atlantic tarpon: can exceed 6 feet, over 200 pounds, jump up to 10 feet in the air.

### Why "Silver King"
Named for the way sunlight reflects off their large, silvery scales (up to 3 inches in diameter) as they leap from the water. The brilliance of the scales earned the regal designation. Name originated 1880-1920.

### The Hooper Trophy
Donated by Col. Hooper, President of the Aransas Pass Tarpon Club. Solid silver base with bronze leaping tarpon. Rules: 5-1/2 foot rod, #9 linen line, and two tarpon over 6 feet in the same year. Designed to match the difficulty of winning the America's Cup.

## FAMOUS VISITORS AND FISHING CLUBS

### Tarpon Inn Notable Guests
- **Franklin D. Roosevelt** (1937) — most famous scale
- **Clyde Beatty** — animal trainer/circus performer
- **Douglas "Wrong Way" Corrigan** — aviator
- **Jesse Jones** — Houston businessman, RFC chairman
- **Hedy Lamarr** — actress and inventor
- **Aimee Semple McPherson** — evangelist (scale displayed)
- **Edward Teller** — physicist ("father of the hydrogen bomb")
- **Duncan Hines** — married and honeymooned at the Inn; recommended it for 25 years

### The Tarpon Club (San Jose Island), 1896
A consortium of wealthy anglers on the tip of San Jose Island. Hundreds of world-class fishermen descended on the area, hiring Mustang Islanders as boatmen. This is how locals learned sport fishing techniques.

### The Tarpon Club of Texas (E.H.R. Green's Club), 1899-1904
- Founded by **Edward Howland Robinson Green** ("Colonel Green"), son of Hetty Green ("the Witch of Wall Street")
- Located on **St. Joseph Island** adjacent to Aransas Pass
- **"The most expensive and exclusive club the world had ever seen"**
- Two-story cypress and pine clubhouse exceeding **12,000 square feet**
- White exterior, green trimmings, red-shingled roof
- **126 incandescent lamps** powered by an electrical plant, visible for miles at sea
- First floor: private offices, billiard rooms, kitchen, dining rooms, dance hall, veranda
- Second floor: 18 sleeping rooms, servant quarters, parlor
- Members called the **"First Four Hundred Sportsmen of America"** — combined wealth in the hundreds of millions
- Activities: tarpon fishing (set records), duck hunting (killed so many waterfowl that sharks gathered nearby)
- Intense press coverage 1898-1902; closed 1904
- Green left Texas in 1910
- Book: "The Tarpon Club of Texas and the Genius of E.H.R. Green" by R.K. Sawyer and Jim Moloney (2023)

### Aransas Pass Tarpon Club
Headquartered in the Tarpon Inn (owned by John Cotter). Col. Hooper was president. Created the Hooper Trophy.

### Port Aransas Boatmen's Association, 1932
25 charter boat and commercial fishermen. Mission: protect fishing waters, sponsor the Texas Tarpon Rodeo. Today: **Port Aransas Boatmen Incorporated**, 501(c)(6) nonprofit, 160+ members. Hosts the Deep Sea Roundup each July.

## THE TARPON INN

### Construction Timeline
- **1886**: Built from surplus Civil War barracks lumber for jetty workers; converted to hotel
- **1900**: Destroyed by fire
- **1904**: Two new structures built
- **1916**: Hit by hurricane
- **1919**: Hurricane (12-15 foot surge) destroyed larger building. Smaller operated until 1926.
- **1923**: James M. Ellis purchased property
- **1925**: Ellis built the standing structure. Pine poles sunk in 16-20 feet of concrete. Roof anchored to poles.
- **1970**: Damaged by Celia. Sold. Closed 5 years.
- **1975**: Reopened under new ownership
- **1979**: National Register of Historic Places. Texas Historic Landmark.
- **2017**: Damaged by Harvey. Tarpon scales survived. Reopened after 8 months.

### The Scale Tradition
- **7,000+ signed tarpon scales** on lobby walls
- Scales 2-3 inches across
- Inscribed with name, catch size, date
- Majority date from the 1930s
- **Oldest**: Roy Milina, 1892
- **Most famous**: FDR's (1937), displayed framed. FDR did not stay at the Inn — slept aboard USS Potomac.

## THE DECLINE

### Timeline
- Fishery peaked 1920s-1940s
- Collapsed in the 1950s, largely complete by early 1960s
- Records show lack of small fish after 1960 while large fish remained — indicates **recruitment failure**, not just reduced adults

### Causes (multiple, compounding)
1. **Adult overfishing**: Mature tarpon routinely killed in tournaments. Sometimes dynamited or netted; carcasses used for fertilizer. Killing 10 per day was common.
2. **Loss of juvenile nursery habitat**: Collapse linked to quality/availability of nursery habitats
3. **1950s Texas drought (1949-1957)**: 30-50% less rain. Led to dam construction — **69 dams built 1957-1970** including Longhorn Dam on Colorado River. Reduced freshwater inflow, nutrients; increased salinity affecting juvenile food sources.
4. **Pesticides**: Identified by Texas Parks & Wildlife as contributing factor

### Tarpon Today
- Still caught June-September, 40-150 pound range
- **Strictly catch-and-release** in Texas
- Fished from jetties, surf, and boats working the beachfront
- Consistent bite returned but nowhere near historical levels
- Harte Research Institute scientists actively studying local population

## ECONOMIC IMPACT

### How Tarpon Shaped Port Aransas
- Tourism cultivated through organized events, guides, angling supply industry starting mid-1920s
- Port Aransas also the nation's **12th largest oil shipping port** into the 1940s

### Population Timeline
- ~100 when renamed Tarpon (1896)
- ~250 by 1896 (post office records)
- 50 in 1925
- 300 in 1931
- ~500 by 1940
- Doubled during WWII (military personnel)
- 824 in 1960
- 1,300 in 1970
- 2,218 in 1982
- **2,904 in 2023**

### Infrastructure Built Around Fishing
- The Tarpon Inn (1886+) — central hub
- Tourist courts and cabins for visiting anglers
- **Ferry system**: First ferry (the Mitzi) carried six cars. Mid-1920s auto ferries opened Port A to car traffic. By 1931, road connections from Aransas Pass to ferry.
- **1932**: Boatmen's Association formed (25 charter captains)
- **1954**: Causeway completed — direct vehicular access
- **1965**: Condo development began (Sea Isle Village)

### The Transition
When tarpon declined in 1960s, fishing economy shifted to broader species. Tarpon Rodeo adapted → Deep Sea Roundup. **2024 tournament (88th edition)**: July 11-14, ~600 entries.

## THE LEGACY

1. **Port Aransas Museum**: Tarpon Era exhibit (opened February 2020) — fishing rods/reels, Rodeo trophies, line-drying spool, tarpon fish print art by Dinah Bowman, silver creamer set from Tarpon Club, mounted tarpon from first Deep Sea Roundup (1932)
2. **The Preserve** (under construction): Maritime museum on Farley campus
3. **The Tarpon Inn**: 7,000+ scales as living memorial
4. **Deep Sea Roundup**: Texas's oldest fishing tournament, annually since 1932
5. **Farley Boat Works**: Physical site preserved and expanding
6. **Cotter Avenue**: Named for Ed Cotter
7. **UT Marine Science Institute**: Founded Port Aransas 1941; Harte Research Institute studying tarpon conservation
