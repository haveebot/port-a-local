## HURRICANE CELIA (AUGUST 3, 1970)

### Timeline

- **July 30, 1970**: Tropical depression forms west of the Cayman Islands.
- **August 1**: Becomes tropical storm entering the Gulf after clipping western Cuba. Rapidly intensifies — pressure drops 25 millibars in 8 hours. Winds reach 115 mph.
- **August 2**: Moves west-northwestward. Weakens slightly to 90 mph in wind shear.
- **August 3, early morning**: Second rapid intensification. Pressure drops 44 millibars in 15 hours (988 mb to 944 mb).
- **August 3, ~4:00 PM CDT**: Makes landfall near Aransas Pass/Port Aransas. **The eye passes directly over Port Aransas.**
- **August 4**: Downgraded to tropical storm over inland Texas.
- **August 5**: Dissipates near Big Bend. Remnants continue into New Mexico.

### Classification and Winds

Debated classification. NWS/Wikipedia cite **Category 3** (125-130 mph sustained). NOAA reanalysis classifies **Category 4** with **140 mph sustained**, minimum central pressure **944 mb**.

Key measurements:
- **Sustained winds**: 125-130 mph at Aransas Pass; 110-140 mph across Nueces County
- **Gusts**: Estimated **180 mph** at Aransas Pass and Robstown
- **Corpus Christi Weather Bureau**: 161 mph gust
- **Taft**: 168 mph gust

The name "Celia" was retired after 1970.

### Storm Surge

Relatively low for a Category 4 (late intensification, compact storm):
- **Port Aransas Beach**: 9.2 feet (highest recorded)
- **Port Aransas Jetty**: 9.0 feet
- **Mustang Island**: 7.9 feet
- **Corpus Christi Bayfront**: 4.9 feet

### The Microburst Phenomenon — What Made Celia Unique

Celia's worst damage was NOT from sustained winds alone. It came through **a series of microbursts and downbursts, most in a 15-minute span**. Survivors described them as sounding like "rocket shells exploding."

Dr. Robert Simpson (National Hurricane Center director) described: "a succession of long streaks of heavy damage...as small pockets of high energy winds radially spaced from north to south at interval lengths of a mile or more, raked across Corpus Christi from west to east."

This is why one house was completely gone while the house next door was untouched. Survivors independently compared it to tornado strikes rather than typical hurricane damage.

## DAMAGE IN PORT ARANSAS

### 75% Figure — VERIFIED
NWS Corpus Christi states **75% of homes and businesses in Port Aransas suffered damage**. For comparison: 70% in Corpus Christi, 90% in Portland.

Regional totals:
- **8,950 homes destroyed**
- **13,850 homes with major damage**
- **41,800 homes with minor damage**
- **252 businesses destroyed**
- **331 boats lost**
- **3,000+ power poles knocked down**

Bill Behrens described Port Aransas: "About one-third of the town had minor damage...In the last third, all that was left was just pilings, the deck and the commode."

### Specific Buildings/Landmarks Damaged
- **Family Center IGA** — Roof completely torn away
- **Port Aransas Mercantile** — Exterior wall destroyed, merchandise still on shelves
- **Custard's Last Stand** (Cotter Ave) — Heavily damaged
- **White Marlin restaurant** — Used as emergency shelter during the storm
- **Seahorse Lodge** — Cottages stripped of roofs
- **Channel View Condominiums** — Broken glass, rain damage throughout

### The Tarpon Inn
**Damaged by Celia.** Took **5 years of repairs** — did not reopen until **1975**. The Ellis family sold after Celia. The famous lobby walls with **7,000+ signed tarpon scales** (including FDR's) survived. Building survived thanks to James M. Ellis's 1925 construction: pine poles sunk in **16-20 feet of concrete**.

### Farley Boat Works
Flying debris during Celia ruined a Farley boat sitting on cement blocks by a house. The 1919 hurricane had earlier motivated the Farleys to move their operation inland from the waterfront.

### The Fishing Fleet
**331 boats lost** across the Coastal Bend:
- **The Marlin Queen** (65-foot party boat) — blown from moorings, floated over parking lot on surge, impaled on pilings as water receded
- Annetta Milina's father's charter boat — **split in half across Cut-off Road**, bow on one side, stern on the other
- Aluminum boats found wrapped around power poles
- Boats blown as far as the Portland Causeway

### The Ferry
- Ferries relocated to Corpus Christi before landfall for protection
- **No ferries operational after the storm**
- **Butch Ousley** piloted his headboat **Lady Lorene** from Port Aransas to Harbor Island to ferry stranded evacuees back
- Bridges along Island Road were washed out — ferry was the lifeline

### Death Toll
- **28 total** (4 Cuba, 8-9 Florida, 15 South Texas)
- **466 injuries** in South Texas
- President Nixon declared a dozen counties disaster areas

### Evacuation
- Forecasters initially predicted Celia would turn northward — some families stayed
- Evacuees scattered to Victoria, Three Rivers, Kingsville, Alice, Corpus Christi
- Returning residents required **vaccinations** before re-entry
- Police barriers at Aransas Pass seawall blocked return; removed under resident pressure

### Recovery
- **Electrical power**: Out for weeks in Port Aransas
- **Water**: No potable water. **Pearl Beer brewery** bottled drinking water in beer bottles for distribution. Volunteers at the ferry landing appeared to be drinking beer but were actually drinking water.
- **Red Cross**: At Community Center distributing ice, water, meals. Tetanus shots.
- **Seventh-day Adventist Church group**: Free hot meals nightly at Community Center
- **National Guard**: Deployed for search-and-rescue
- **Federal trailers**: FEMA trailers distributed. Jane Thompson lived in a Mobile Scout camper for 5 months.
- **Community barbecues**: Residents cooked freezer food communally before it spoiled
- Town generally stabilized by **1972** (two years)
- Conditions: extreme heat, no AC, sleeping outdoors, intense mosquitoes, sand covering every street

### Economic Impact
- **Total damage**: $930 million (1970 USD) — approximately **$3.1 billion in 2020 dollars**
- At the time, **costliest tropical cyclone in Texas history** (until Hurricane Alicia 1983)
- Many Port Aransas residents could not afford restoration and abandoned properties

## THE 1919 HURRICANE — The Worst in Port A History

- Part of the 1919 Florida Keys Hurricane. Texas landfall at Baffin Bay, September 14.
- Winds at least **110 mph**; storm surge **11.1 feet** (some sources say 12-15 feet)
- **"The whole town was gone! Every single house and store, hotel and inn!"**
- Oil tanks ruptured; victims swept into the bay coated in heavy crude oil, making identification impossible
- Only a school building survived (near present-day Dairy Queen, 300 block West Cotter Avenue)
- Wind lasted 18 hours continuously
- A communal shelter housing 100+ people collapsed after filling with water
- Survivors fled to Cedar Hill (near present Beach Street)
- One survivor: "My arms and legs were swollen 3 or 4 times their actual size and I felt as though every bit of skin on my body had been stripped off."
- Rescue delayed 3 days — Corpus Christi authorities believed there were no survivors
- **Death toll**: At least 284 in Texas. NWS estimates **600-1,000 may have died** — many victims in Nueces Bay, coated in crude oil, never identified
- Most families relocated after the storm. Port Aransas was essentially **depopulated and had to be resettled**.

## OTHER MAJOR STORMS

### 1916 Hurricane (August 18)
- Destroyed Port Aransas "except for a few buildings"
- Island flooded and infested with rattlesnakes
- Corpus Christi: $1.5 million damage, 20 deaths

### Hurricane Carla (1961)
- Destroyed most of Port Aransas's "illegal joints" — never rebuilt
- Ended the town's reputation as a lawless party destination

### Hurricane Harvey (August 25-26, 2017)
- **Category 4**, 130 mph winds at landfall near Port Aransas/Rockport
- **100% of the town's businesses damaged**
- **85% of homes damaged**
- City infrastructure damage: **$50-70 million**
- Storm surge: at least **6 feet**
- Salt water infiltrated gas lines throughout entire city
- Mayor **Charles Bujan** called evacuation for ~4,000 residents on August 24
- Over **$170 million** in state/federal recovery money to Nueces County
- Tarpon Inn: 8-foot surge, building held, tarpon scales survived. Reopened after **8 months**.
- **Affordable housing destroyed**, forcing low-income residents to permanently relocate to mainland

### The Pattern
Port Aransas has experienced **43 hurricanes since 1930**. The book **"Hurricane Junction: A History of Port Aransas"** by Cyril Matthew Kuehne (1973, St. Mary's University) takes its title from this.

Major destructive sequence:
1. **1916** — Town nearly wiped out
2. **1919** — Town completely wiped out; depopulated
3. **1961 (Carla)** — Significant damage; social transformation
4. **1970 (Celia)** — 75% damaged; 5-year Tarpon Inn closure
5. **2017 (Harvey)** — 100% of businesses, 85% of homes damaged

## THE RESILIENCE STORY

Jane Thompson (Celia survivor): "Everyone pulled together, neighbors jumped in and helped each other."

**The dark side of resilience**: Harvey permanently changed Port Aransas's demographics. Destruction of affordable housing forced low-income residents off the island permanently. Each rebuilding cycle makes the town more expensive and less accessible.
