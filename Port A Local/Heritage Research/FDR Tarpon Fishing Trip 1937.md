## THE TRIP: FULL ITINERARY

### Political Context

FDR needed this trip badly. He was embroiled in the "court-packing" crisis — the Judicial Procedures Reform Bill of 1937, introduced in February, which proposed expanding the Supreme Court from 9 to as many as 15 justices to overcome conservative opposition to New Deal legislation. Despite his landslide 1936 reelection, the plan was generating fierce political headwinds. The trip was meant to give FDR 10 days "off the mortal clock."

### The Entourage

FDR traveled with:
- **Elliott Roosevelt** — FDR's second son (who had visited Port Aransas in 1936, possibly scouting it for his father)
- **Admiral Ross T. McIntire** — FDR's personal physician
- **Edwin M. "Pa" Watson** — military aide
- **Captain Paul H. Bastedo** — captain of the presidential yacht USS Potomac
- **Marvin McIntyre** — appointments secretary
- Secret Service detail (not individually named in sources)

### Day-by-Day Timeline

**April 27, 1937** — After dinner, FDR left the White House for his special train.

**April 28** — Train departed Washington at 6:00 AM, heading south.

**April 29** — Train arrived in New Orleans in the afternoon. FDR had a late lunch at **Antoine's Restaurant** with Louisiana Governor Richard Leche and New Orleans Mayor Robert Maestri. A waterfront press conference followed. The famous anecdote: Mayor Maestri supposedly asked FDR, "How ya like dem ersters?" That evening the party boarded the destroyer **USS Moffett**, anchored in the Mississippi River.

**April 30** — USS Moffett departed early morning into the Gulf of Mexico, escorted by **USS Decatur**.

**May 1** — Arrived off **Aransas Pass**. FDR and party immediately began fishing outside the jetties. They then transferred to the presidential yacht **USS Potomac**, anchored in the mouth of the **Lydia Ann Channel**. During the transfer, FDR went fishing from a small boat and snagged a king mackerel.

**May 2 (Sunday)** — FDR did not fish on the Sabbath. Elliott Roosevelt, however, caught a **five-foot, ten-inch tarpon** this day.

**May 3** — Serious fishing began around the jetties with Port Aransas guides enlisted. The party caught **12 tarpons** from the fishing boats. FDR caught his first tarpon off the south jetty.

**May 4** — Early cruise south to **Port Isabel**. FDR fished for speckled trout — caught none. On the return journey, he did well trolling for **kingfish**.

**May 5** — Potomac steamed back to Port Aransas. More kingfish trolling on the way.

**May 6** — Potomac anchored again in Lydia Ann Channel. FDR attended to affairs of state. No fishing.

**May 7** — FDR toured the **game preserve on St. Joseph Island** (San Jose Island), owned by **Sid Richardson**, the Texas oil wildcatter. The island featured longhorn cattle, buffalo, and hunting grounds. The famous **bull chute anecdote**: There was no proper dock, so Richardson proposed using a cattle loading chute to get wheelchair-bound FDR onto the island. FDR said: "What in the world Sid; do you mean you're going to roll me down that bull chute?" Richardson replied: "Why, Mr. President, you're the biggest bull that ever went down that chute!" After St. Joseph Island, the group crossed **Cedar Bayou** at low tide to reach **Clint Murchison's** 13,000-square-foot home on **Matagorda Island** for mint juleps on the veranda and jackrabbit hunting. FDR reciprocated with dinner aboard the Potomac that evening for Richardson, Murchison, and their associates.

**May 8 (Saturday)** — FDR's **final fishing day**. He fished with local guides **Ted Mathews** (pilot) and **Barney Farley Sr.** (boat handler). At **3:27 PM**, FDR boated a **five-foot, 77-pound tarpon** — a "silver king" — approximately half a mile off the bow of the Potomac. Newsreel photographers and press in a nearby boat captured the moment. Barney Farley is shown in the iconic photo with Elliott Roosevelt behind him, holding up the tarpon. **Sid Richardson paid the guide fees.**

**May 9 (Sunday)** — Potomac took on water at the Engineers Pier, then steamed through the pass, beginning FDR's journey home.

**May 11** — Potomac arrived in **Galveston** at 8:00 AM to a **21-gun salute from Fort Crockett**. FDR met newly elected 28-year-old Congressman **Lyndon B. Johnson** and Texas Governor **James Allred** on the dock. LBJ had just won a special election running on a pro-court-packing platform. LBJ rode with FDR on the train from Galveston to **College Station**, then FDR continued to Fort Worth to board a train to Washington.

**May 14** — FDR's train arrived at Washington, D.C. rail station at **10:15 AM**. He was met by **Eleanor Roosevelt** and returned to the White House with Mr. and Mrs. Elliott Roosevelt.

## THE FISHING

### The Guides

**Barney Farley Sr.** — Arrived in Port Aransas from Florida in **1910** at age 16 (born approximately 1894). He came to visit relatives and stayed to become a tarpon fishing guide. He worked Texas coastal waters out of Port Aransas for **more than half a century**. He was the brother of Fred Farley (the boat builder). Barney became a leader and prominent businessman in Port Aransas's sport fishing industry. He wrote a memoir, **"Fishing Yesterday's Gulf Coast"**, originally recorded in the 1960s for his family, published posthumously in **2008** by Texas A&M University Press (Gulf Coast Books series, Volume 3). The book chronicles changes in sport and commercial fishing along the Central Texas Coast from 1910 through the 1960s. His grandson, **Barney Farley III**, still lives in Port Aransas.

**Ted Mathews** — Served as the pilot on FDR's final fishing day (May 8). Local Port Aransas guide.

### The Boat

The guide boat was likely called **"Hangover II"** — one source references a photo caption identifying it as "probably from the guide boat 'Hangover II.'" After an unsuccessful initial outing on FDR's own 35-foot fishing boat, **Barney Farley convinced his nephew Don Farley** to bring the president aboard a **Farley Boat**. FDR caught so many tarpon that (according to the Farley Boats Wikipedia article) he returned to Port Aransas later that year.

### The Catch — Full Record

- **May 1**: King mackerel (during transfer to Potomac)
- **May 2**: Elliott Roosevelt caught a 5'10" tarpon; FDR did not fish (Sunday)
- **May 3**: The party caught 12 tarpons total; FDR caught his first tarpon off the south jetty
- **May 4**: No trout at Port Isabel; kingfish on return trip
- **May 5**: Kingfish trolling en route back to Port Aransas
- **May 8**: FDR's trophy catch — **five-foot, 77-pound tarpon at 3:27 PM** with guides Mathews and Farley

Note: One source lists the tarpon at 80 pounds; the majority cite 77 pounds.

## THE TARPON SCALE AT THE TARPON INN

FDR signed a scale from his May 8 tarpon. That **signed tarpon scale is displayed in a frame on a wall in the lobby/office of the Tarpon Inn**. It remains there today — it survived **Hurricane Harvey in 2017** (the lobby walls with the scales were spared despite 8-foot storm surge flooding the bottom floor). The inn reopened after 8 months of repairs.

Notably, **FDR did not actually stay at the Tarpon Inn** — he slept aboard the USS Potomac. But he followed the local custom of signing a scale.

## THE TARPON INN

### Construction History

- **1886**: Frank Stephenson, boat pilot and assistant Aransas Lighthouse Keeper, opened the original inn in surplus Civil War barracks. Named it "Tarpon Inn" for the abundant fish.
- **1897**: Mary Cotter and her son J.E. bought the inn from Stephenson.
- **1900**: Destroyed by fire.
- **1904**: Two replacement structures built.
- **1919**: Hurricane with 12-15 foot storm surge destroyed the larger building.
- **1923**: James M. Ellis and wife purchased the property.
- **1925**: Ellis built the structure that stands today, designed to resemble the original barracks. He sank **20-foot pine poles into 16 feet of concrete** with pilings at each room corner, with the roof anchored to these poles for hurricane resistance.
- **1970**: Hurricane Celia caused damage; Ellis family sold.
- **1975**: Reopened under new ownership.
- **1979**: Added to the **National Register of Historic Places** (September 14, 1979). Texas Historical Commission marker erected.
- **2017**: Hurricane Harvey — survived with damage to windows, doors, and floors. The lobby and tarpon scales were preserved. Reopened after 8 months.

### The Tarpon Scale Tradition

The lobby walls display **over 7,000 signed tarpon scales**, each bearing the angler's autograph, hometown, and the size, weight, and date of catch. The oldest scale on display dates to either **1892** or **July 1897** depending on source.

### Notable Guests Who Signed Scales

- **Franklin D. Roosevelt** (1937) — most famous scale
- **Clyde Beatty** — animal trainer
- **Douglas "Wrong Way" Corrigan** — aviator
- **Jesse Jones** — Houston businessman, FDR's Secretary of Commerce
- **Hedy Lamarr** — actress and inventor
- **Aimee Semple McPherson** — evangelist
- **Edward Teller** — physicist (father of the hydrogen bomb)
- **Duncan Hines** — food critic; was **married at the Tarpon Inn** and honeymooned there, recommending it for 25 years afterward

## THE FARLEY FAMILY

### Fred vs. Barney — Relationship

Fred and Barney Farley were **brothers**, not father and son.

- **Barney Farley** arrived in Port Aransas from Florida in **1910** at age 16. He became a fishing guide.
- **Charles Frederick "Fred" Farley** was a master craftsman living in Birmingham, Alabama. Barney told Fred that Port Aransas guides needed powerboats for tarpon fishing. Fred traveled to the coast and settled in Port Aransas.
- Fred established **"Farley and Son, Boat Builders"** in **1915** with his son.
- Fred's sons **Jim Farley** and **Fred Farley Jr.** continued the business.
- The **1919 hurricane** devastated the waterfront and motivated the Farleys to move operations inland.
- Post-WWII, operations moved to Mercer Street, then to Avenue C around 1968.
- The family built boats until **1975**, never using fiberglass.
- Boats were built from **5/8-inch-thick top-grade cypress planks** (switched to mahogany post-WWII when cypress became scarce).
- The Farleys **never drew up official plans** — they marked designs directly on the wood flooring of their shop.

### Don Farley

Barney's nephew **Don Farley** provided his Farley Boat when FDR's own vessel proved unsuccessful. This is what led to FDR catching tarpon from a Farley-built craft.

## HART STILWELL

Texas newspaperman **Hart Stilwell** (1902-1975) was present and fished with FDR during the trip. He was an outdoor writer for Esquire, Field & Stream, Outdoor Life, and Sports Afield. His nearly finished manuscript was posthumously published as **"Glory of the Silver King: The Golden Age of Tarpon Fishing"** (2011, Texas A&M University Press, Gulf Coast Books Vol. 19).
