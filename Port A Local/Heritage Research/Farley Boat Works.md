## THE FOUNDERS

### Charles Frederick "Fred" Farley
- **Born:** August 19, 1878, Birmingham, Alabama
- **Died:** December 18, 1936
- Find A Grave memorial: #67109077
- Master craftsman / experienced woodworker
- Before Port Aransas: lived in Rockport, TX. Earlier traveled from Alabama to Gulf Coast with his brothers, designing and building utility boats, lighthouses, and ornate bars.
- Moved his wife **Mabel** and sons to Port Aransas in **1914** to establish the boat works.

### Barney Farley Sr.
- Fred's brother (confirmed — brothers, not father/son)
- Arrived Port Aransas circa **1910** as an aspiring tarpon fishing guide
- Became famous tarpon guide and prominent civic leader
- Guided FDR on May 8, 1937 (77-pound tarpon at 3:27 PM)
- Wrote memoir: **"Fishing Yesterday's Gulf Coast"** (Texas A&M, 2008)
- **Barney Farley III** (grandson) still resides in Port Aransas
- Exact birth/death dates not found — gap requiring family or PAPHA contact

### Other Brothers
Sources mention Fred traveled to the Gulf Coast "with his three brothers" — at least four Farley brothers total. Only Fred and Barney are prominently associated with Port Aransas.

### Next Generation
- **Jim Farley** — Fred's son, boat builder (built the Tina)
- **Fred Farley Jr.** — Fred's son, boat builder
- **Don Farley** — described variously as son or nephew. Captained boats for FDR's visit.

### Keith Farley
- Grandnephew of Fred. Has gathered documents, articles, conducted interviews to chronicle family history. Gave talks at PAPHA events. Helped locate the Tina in Rockport. **Traces the Farley family in America back to 1622** (arrival from England).

## THE BOAT WORKS (1914-1975)

### Founding
- Established **1914** (Fred arrived and set up shop)
- First boat launched **1915** — first Farley boat for tarpon fishing
- Business name: **"Farley and Son, Boat Builders"**

### What Prompted It
Barney, already guiding from Port Aransas, saw that local guides desperately needed reliable, reasonably priced powerboats for tarpon fishing in the choppy Aransas Pass conditions. He wrote to Fred about the untapped market.

### Four Locations
1. **Original waterfront** (1914-1919) — harbor shoreline, SE of Coast Guard station. Destroyed by **1919 hurricane** (12-15 ft surge).
2. **Inland, east of Tarpon Inn** (post-1919) — shellcrete siding fortification
3. **White Street / Mercer Street** (post-WWII through 1960s) — Jim Farley built the Tina here
4. **716 W. Avenue C** (circa 1968 to closure) — the final location. PAPHA purchased 2011.

After Farleys closed: building housed **Steve's Boat Works**, then **Camric Boats**, before PAPHA.

### The No-Blueprint Method
- **Never created formal plans or blueprints**
- Designs sketched directly on the **wood flooring** using colored carpenter's crayon
- Prospective buyers viewed designs drawn on plywood
- Custom modifications demonstrated through **hand-carved half-models**
- Each boat essentially custom-built

### Hull Design
- **V-bottom hulls** — optimized for choppy Aransas Pass waters
- **Hard chine** design (angular, not rounded)
- Chine doesn't touch water until well aft of bow
- **Low sides (gunwales)** with rounded tumblehomes — easier to land tarpon
- **High bows** — combat choppy seas
- Open cockpit with stern-facing fighting chairs
- Front windshield with opening hatch
- Low cabins (when included) allowing 360-degree fishing access

### Size and Materials
- **16 to 28 feet** (original signature: 18-footer for two people)
- **Pre-WWII**: 5/8-inch-thick top-grade cypress planks (light and durable)
- **Post-WWII**: Honduran and Philippine mahogany (cypress scarce)
- **Engines**: Converted automobile engines — Chrysler flatheads, Ford, Chevrolet. Not marine motors. Some customers supplied their own engines.

### Types Built (Evolution)
1. **Tarpon fishing boats** (core product) — open cockpit, 16-28 ft
2. **Speedboats** (1920s+) — including the Munsill commission
3. **Cabin-equipped boats** — for duck hunting guides
4. **Corporate/VIP models** — for wealthy clientele
5. **Offshore cruisers** (1950s+) — pivoted when tarpon declined

### Notable: The Munsill Speedboat (~1928)
- Built for **Gail Borden Munsill** of the Borden dairy empire
- Capable of **60 mph**
- Proved Farley skills extended far beyond fishing craft

### "First Production Sport Fishing Boats in the U.S." Claim
Appears consistently across PAPHA, Wooden Boat Festival, and journalism. Ashley Harris (COO of PAPHA): "arguably the first sportfishing vessel built on the Gulf Coast, if not the first built in the United States." No formal dispute found. Safe to present as "widely regarded as."

### The FDR Connection (1937)
- After unsuccessful outings on his own 35-foot vessel, a local captain suggested switching to a Farley boat
- May 8, 1937: FDR fished with Barney Farley Sr. and Ted Mathews on a Farley boat
- 3:27 PM: landed a 77-pound, 5-foot tarpon
- FDR was so pleased he reportedly returned later that same year

### The Chiropractic Tarpon (1935)
Dr. Richard Sutton, aboard Don Farley's boat, was struck in the neck by a leaping tarpon. Sutton credited the impact with curing his chronic neck pain — famously calling it "The Chiropractic Tarpon."

### Closure (1973-1975)
Sources vary on exact year. Cause: rise of fiberglass boats made mass-produced synthetic vessels cheaper. The Farleys could not compete. Family quote: **"God wanted fiberglass boats, He would have made fiberglass trees."**

## NOTABLE BOATS AND ARTIFACTS

### "Tina" — The Only Complete Original Farley Hull
- **Builder**: Jim Farley, at the White Street location
- **Built for**: Sam Cone (wealthy angler)
- **Named after**: Cone's wife, Tina
- **Date**: ~1947 (sources vary; 1947 most consistent)
- **History**: After Sam and Tina divorced, Cone stored the boat in a barn at Avenue B and Trojan Street for ~50 years.
- **Chain of ownership**: Rick Pratt → David Loese (Buda) → Steve Loese (Bastrop) → Jeff Morehouse (San Antonio, restoration by Ron Blue in Rockport) → Ned Teller
- **Return**: Keith Farley located it in Rockport. With Barney Farley III, John Studeman, and Thomas Teller — transported back to Port Aransas **May 26, 2007**
- Refurbished **2012**. Currently on display at Farley Boat Works. Will move to The Preserve.

### "The Starfish"
- **28-foot** original Farley boat from the **late 1960s**
- Donated in poor condition
- Under major restoration (PAPHA received **$20,000 Brown Foundation grant**)
- Visitors can observe restoration from elevated platform

### "Lydia Ann" — Gulf Coast Scow Schooner
- **60-foot** replica of a Texas Gulf Coast scow schooner (flat-bottomed cargo vessels, 1870s-1920s)
- Named for the Lydia Ann Channel
- Originated at **Galveston and Trinity Bay Marine Museum** (2001). Stalled after **Hurricane Ike (2008)** diverted all funds to the tall ship Elissa.
- **2013**: Transferred to PAPHA
- **2015**: Construction resumed at Farley Boat Works. **Dan Pecore** leads the build team.
- Current: 57-foot hull, 13-foot breadth, 37-foot main hull and masts completed. Rudder unfinished, rigging not commenced.
- Survived **Hurricane Harvey (2017)** nearly unscathed
- When completed: **the only Texas scow schooner in existence anywhere**
- Vision: moored in city marina for historical tours through the jetties

### Other Artifacts
- Antique shipbuilding tools (original Farley equipment)
- Ship binnacles (large ship compasses)
- Outboard motor collection (early 1900s)
- Historical photographs from the tarpon era
- The Ninth Street building whose roof IS the last boat ever built at Farley Boat Works (survived Harvey)

## THE REVIVAL (2011-Present)

### Purchase and Reopening
- **2011**: PAPHA purchased 716 W. Avenue C
- Building in deteriorated condition; renovated, machine shop retooled
- **February 7, 2012**: Official reopening

### Doyle Marek — The Bridge Between Eras
- **Born**: 1932, Robstown, TX
- **Died**: March 25, 2017, Kerrville, TX (age 84)
- Taught at Port Aransas ISD for **36 years** (1955-1991)
- Created boat-building program at PA schools (~1970s-1990). Students built **51 wooden boats** winning **15 awards**.
- **2012**: Provided inaugural instruction at revived Farley Boat Works, free of charge, to 40+ volunteers. With friend **Will Mayfield**, built two skiffs. One sold at auction; the other (**"Lady Jo Ann"**, named after Chris Page's wife) remains on display.
- The **"Marek Skiff" / "Port A Skiff"** — signature boat of the revival — named for him.
- PAHS gymnasium named in his honor
- Boatman Hall of Fame (1997)

### Key Leadership
- **Rick Pratt** — Museum Director 2009-2018. Spearheaded the revival. Found and purchased the Tina. The boat-building wing was officially named the **"Rick Pratt School of Wooden Boat Building"** in his honor.
- **Cliff Strain** — Executive Director post-Pratt era
- **Ashley Harris** — COO of PAPHA
- **Frank Colella** — Manager ~4 years, retired July 2018
- **Dan Pecore** — Took over 2018; leads Lydia Ann construction
- **Frank Cabrera** — Manager as of 2024

### What Gets Built Today
- **"Port A Skiff" / "Marek Skiff"** — flat-bottomed plywood fishing boats, 14-20 feet, shallow-water
- Kayaks, paddleboards, rowboats (QT rowboats, mouse boats)
- Kit boats (Classic 19 Express)
- Core Sound 20 sailboats
- Scamp sailboats
- Participants can bring own boat kits for assembly

### Production (Revival Era)
**130+ wooden boats** built since 2011. Ranges from simple kits (~3 days) to skiffs (~2 months) to complex builds.

### The Volunteer Builders
- Core of dedicated volunteers, many **Winter Texans**
- Described as a "husband daycare center" by spouses
- From across U.S. and internationally
- Shop charges for space/materials but provides **free instruction**

### Current Operations

## THE PRESERVE AT FARLEY BOAT WORKS

### Overview
- **6,000+ sq ft** maritime museum under construction on W. Avenue C
- Inspired by the **old U.S. Lifesaving Station** (built Port Aransas 1876)
- Architect: **David L. Walker** (also designed Port Aransas Art Center)
- L-shaped: air-conditioned exhibit space + unairconditioned boat display wing
- Includes bathrooms, office, gift shop
- Event space: lectures, fundraisers, weddings, private rentals

### What It Will House
- The Tina and The Starfish
- David McKee Antique Tackle Collection
- Antique outboard motors, ship compasses
- Maps, charts, historical photographs
- Educational exhibits on wooden boat building

### The McKee Collection
- **Dr. David McKee** of Corpus Christi. Started collecting at **age 12**.
- First major donation: **October 2022**
- **Hundreds of antique rods, reels, lures** — some pre-1900
- **40+ boxes** of vintage tackle
- Believed to be **one of the largest vintage fishing tackle collections on public display in the U.S.**

### Timeline
- **May 7, 2021**: Groundbreaking (followed by fundraising gala at Tarpon Inn)
- Funded partly by **FEMA Harvey grant** (two hurricane-damaged boat barns → one new structure)
- Early 2024: concrete, metal shell, doors, windows complete. Stalled by FEMA delays.
- Late 2023/2024: **Mustang Homes of Port Aransas** hired for finish-out
- **Projected**: within 6-12 months as of Feb 2024. No confirmed opening found. Verify with PAPHA.
