## 2. Port Aransas Museum

### The Building

- **Address:** 408 N. Alister Street, Port Aransas, TX 78373
- **Phone:** (361) 749-3800
- **Building history:** A kit/mail-order house shipped to Port Aransas circa 1915-1916. After the 1916 hurricane destroyed the original Life Saving Station, this kit house served as the replacement station until a new Coast Guard station opened in 1925. Sold into private sector in 1930s. Passed through several prominent Port Aransas families. The Morrison family of San Antonio donated it to PAPHA.
- **Museum opened:** 2008 (concept to opening in under three years)
- **Created by:** Joint effort between PAPHA and the City of Port Aransas
- **Admission:** Free; docent-led tours available
- **Only second director in history:** Rick Pratt (2009-2018), then Cliff Strain (2018-present)

### Permanent Exhibits / Highlights

- **Fourth Order Fresnel Lens** — Originally installed in Lydia Ann Lighthouse from 1878 to 1952. Made in the 1860s by Augustin Henry Lapaute. 2-foot-4-inch tall. Decommissioned by Coast Guard in 1954; city turned it over to museum after its 2008 opening.
- **Jetty train bell** — From the train that brought rocks to build the Port Aransas jetties (completed 1919)
- **1920s/1930s films** — Rare film footage of Port Aransas in the 1920s and 1930s, available for viewing and purchase
- **Oral histories** — Video interviews with important islanders; ongoing Oral Histories Project capturing stories of hurricanes, fishing, local characters

### Rotating / Named Exhibits

- **"The Tarpon Era"** — Opened end of February 2020. Chronicles the era from 1880s through late 1950s. Items include:
  - Tarpon rods and reels
  - Tarpon Rodeo/Deep Sea Roundup trophies
  - Linen line-drying spool
  - Tarpon fish print art by Dinah Bowman
  - Sugar and creamer set from the Tarpon Club
  - Mounted tarpon from the first Deep Sea Roundup (1932) — believed caught by Totsy Millican (though entered under husband North Millican's name)
- **"Taming the Channel"** — Exhibit about the construction of the Port Aransas jetties (completed 1919)
- **"100 Years of Sport Fishing in Port Aransas"** — Past exhibit
- **Hurricane/Harvey content** — The museum documents repeated community resilience through the 1916 hurricane, Hurricane Celia (1970), Hurricane Harvey (2017). Displays include a 1940s Farley boat wrecked by Celia.

### Collections / Archive

- **25,500+ archived photographs and documents** pertaining to Port Aransas history
- Oral history recordings (ongoing project)
- 1920s film footage
- Maritime artifacts, fishing tackle, navigational instruments
- Archive described as "publicly accessible database" — photos available for viewing and purchase

- **Facebook pages** for Port Aransas Museum and Farley Boat Works — standard social media presence
- Photos described as available "for viewing and purchase" — unclear if this means in-person only or if any online portal exists

### The Gap

- **Almost none of the 25,500+ photos appear to be digitized or accessible online**
- **No oral histories are published digitally** (no podcast, no YouTube, no audio archive)
- **1920s film footage not available online** in any form found
- **No virtual exhibits or digital storytelling**
- **No searchable online collection database**

- This represents a massive heritage content opportunity: a physical collection of extraordinary depth with essentially zero digital reach

## 6. The Preserve at Farley Boat Works

- **What it is:** A 6,000-square-foot maritime museum being constructed adjacent to the existing Farley Boat Works work sheds at 716 W. Avenue C
- **Architect:** David L. Walker of Corpus Christi (also designed the Port Aransas Art Center)
- **Design:** L-shaped structure; air-conditioned main exhibition space + unheated wing. Exterior of one section will replicate the historic U.S. Lifesaving Station from the early 1900s. Will include bathrooms, office, gift shop, event rental space.
- **PAPHA board approved architect:** September 2020
- **Ground broken:** May 2021 (originally targeted early 2021)
- **Funding:** FEMA grant — the original two storage structures/boat barns on the property were destroyed by Hurricane Harvey (2017). FEMA program allowed combining funds from two demolished buildings to finance Phase One. One specific FEMA allocation of ~$16,000 noted for boat restoration.
- **Construction status (as of February 2024 South Jetty article):**
  - Shell and concrete slab completed; doors, windows, some electrical installed
  - Work had stalled for several months
  - **Mustang Homes of Port Aransas** hired in late 2023 for finish-out work
  - Finish-out scope: flooring, interior walls, ceiling, electrical equipment, HVAC, sprinkler system, office, restrooms, expansion onto existing front porch
  - Ashley Harris estimated opening in 6-12 months (i.e., August 2024 - February 2025)
  - Delays attributed to FEMA bureaucracy and contractor availability
- **Current status (April 2026):** Unknown — no subsequent articles found confirming opening. This is a key gap to investigate.

### Planned Collections for The Preserve

- **The McKee Collection (Dr. David McKee):** Described as "one of the most complete collections of offshore/inshore tackle on display anywhere." All rods are wooden (split bamboo and cane), most restored and fitted with age-appropriate reels. 400+ antique fishing rods and reels, 40+ boxes of vintage tackle. Claimed as potentially one of the largest public displays of antique tackle in the U.S.
- Two original complete Farley boats: the **Starfish** and the **Tina**
- Vintage outboard motor collection
- Compass and binnacle collection
- Maritime maps and charts
- Boats, tools, and other artifacts
- Elevated platform planned for public observation of boat restoration work
- Event rental space for PAPHA and external organizations

## 8. Coast Guard Station / Lifesaving Station History

### Timeline

| Year | Event |
|---|---|
| 1878 | First Aransas Life Saving Station established on Mustang Island at Cotter and Station Streets |
| 1915 | U.S. Life Saving Service merged into newly formed U.S. Coast Guard |
| 1916 | Original station destroyed by hurricane. The kit house (now the museum at 408 N. Alister) served as temporary replacement |
| 1919 | Another devastating hurricane |
| 1925 | Second Life Saving / Coast Guard station opened; kit house sold to private sector |
| 1925-1976 | Second station in continuous service |
| 1976 | Second station replaced due to foundation erosion and increased workload |
| 2017 | Hurricane Harvey causes extensive damage to Coast Guard station |
| 2019 (April) | Harvey-damaged Coast Guard station demolished |
| 2022 | New Coast Guard facility construction completed |

### Connection to Museum

The museum building (the ~1915 kit house) served as the Life Saving Station/Coast Guard station from 1916 to 1925 — a direct physical link between the museum space and the Coast Guard heritage. This is a powerful storytelling element: visitors are literally standing in a building that once housed the Coast Guard.

## 9. Educational Programs

### Rick Pratt School of Wooden Boat Building (at Farley Boat Works)

- Named by PAPHA board vote, November 5, 2012
- Open to public participation — visitors can observe and join in
- **120+ wooden boats built since 2011** (as of most recent reporting)
- Skiff-building classes: typically Tuesday-Saturday, 8am-5pm, producing 16-foot, 5-foot beam, tiller-operated outboard skiffs
- Spring Break workshops: visitors build wooden skiffs during Spring Break week
- Children and teen programs: local school students learn woodworking, build items to take home

### Volunteer Builder Culture

- Off-season: Winter Texans (seasonal residents) make up majority of volunteers
- Year-round volunteer builders welcome; contact Frankie Cabrera (current Boat Building Manager)
- The boat shop is a "living exhibit" — sounds of woodworking greet visitors immediately

### Doyle Marek's Legacy

- High school shop class boat building program (late 1970s-~1990)
- The "School Skiff" / "Marek Skiff" / Port A Skiff tradition
- Returned from retirement in 2012 to launch the reconstituted Farley program
- First class: 40+ students, two skiffs built with help from friend Will Mayfield

### Museum Education

- Docent-led tours at Port Aransas Museum
- Oral Histories Project (recording community members)
- Film screenings (1920s/1930s footage)
- Rotating exhibits with interpretive content

## 10. Future Vision

### The Preserve Opening
- The single biggest near-term milestone is completing and opening The Preserve at Farley Boat Works. As of the last public report (Feb 2024), it was 6-12 months out. Current status unknown — may be open or near opening by now (April 2026).

### Collection Growth
- The McKee Collection and other maritime artifacts will have proper permanent display space for the first time.
- Event rental capability at The Preserve creates a revenue stream.

### Lydia Ann Schooner Project
- **60-foot** replica of a Texas Gulf Coast scow schooner (flat-bottomed cargo vessels, 1870s-1920s)
- Named for the Lydia Ann Channel
- Originated at **Galveston and Trinity Bay Marine Museum** (2001). Stalled after **Hurricane Ike (2008)** diverted all funds to the tall ship Elissa.
- **2013**: Transferred to PAPHA
- **2015**: Construction resumed at Farley Boat Works. **Dan Pecore** leads the build team.
- Current: 57-foot hull, 13-foot breadth, 37-foot main hull and masts completed. Rudder unfinished, rigging not commenced.
- Survived **Hurricane Harvey (2017)** nearly unscathed
- When completed: **the only Texas scow schooner in existence anywhere**
- Vision: moored in city marina for historical tours through the jetties

### Expansion Trajectory
- PAPHA has grown from a small preservation society (founded 2002) to managing multiple properties with $2M+ in assets
- Pattern of steady acquisition: museum (2008), Farley Boat Works property (2011), The Preserve (ground broken 2021)
- Digital expansion is the obvious next frontier (see Digital Presence Gap above)

## CONTENT HOOKS (Heritage Storytelling Angles)

1. **"The Building That Was the Coast Guard"** — The museum literally occupies a 1915 kit house that served as the Coast Guard station. Visitors stand in the same space where lifesavers worked. A mail-order house shipped to an island, survived a hurricane, became a government facility, became family homes, became a museum. That is a Texas story.

2. **"25,000 Photos Nobody Can See"** — The digital presence gap is the hook. A small-town museum sitting on one of the most significant coastal heritage photo archives in Texas, with 1920s film footage and oral histories — almost none of it accessible beyond the physical walls. The case for digitization practically writes itself.

3. **"120 Boats and Counting"** — From Doyle Marek's high school shop class to the Rick Pratt School, Port Aransas has an unbroken (if sometimes interrupted) tradition of teaching ordinary people to build wooden boats. The volunteer builder culture — especially Winter Texans — is a human interest goldmine.

4. **"FDR Fished in a Farley"** — President Roosevelt chose Farley boats for his 1937 fishing trip to Port Aransas. The Starfish and the Tina are the only two original complete Farley boats known to survive.

5. **"Totsy's Tarpon"** — The mounted tarpon from the first Deep Sea Roundup (1932) was entered under North Millican's name but believed caught by his wife Totsy. A women-in-fishing angle with nearly a century of history behind it.

6. **"The McKee Collection Nobody Knows About"** — 400+ antique rods and reels, 40+ boxes of tackle, potentially one of the largest public displays in the U.S. — and it has essentially zero online presence. One webpage. No catalog. No images.

7. **"From Tarpon Capital to Harvey and Back"** — The museum documents repeated cycles of destruction and rebuilding: 1916, 1919, Celia (1970), Harvey (2017). The resilience narrative is the through-line of Port Aransas history.

8. **"The Lens That Guided Ships for 74 Years"** — The Fourth Order Fresnel lens (1878-1952) by Augustin Henry Lapaute, now the museum's centerpiece. A physical artifact connecting visitors to 19th-century maritime navigation.

9. **"A Six-Thousand-Square-Foot Promise"** — The Preserve's long construction saga (ground broken 2021, still finishing as of 2024) against FEMA bureaucracy and contractor shortages is a story about small-town determination.

10. **"The Farley Family Boats That Built a Town"** — Fred Farley arrived 1914, founded the boat works, his sons carried it on until 1973 when fiberglass killed the wooden boat trade. PAPHA bought the property in 2011 and brought the tradition back. Barney Farley III (grand-nephew) provides the living family connection.
