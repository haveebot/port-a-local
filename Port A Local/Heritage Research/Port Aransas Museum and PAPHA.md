- **501(c)(3) status:** Designated since November 2005
- **EIN:** 71-0926680
- **Category:** Arts, Culture and Humanities / Historical Societies
- **Registered address:** 716 W Avenue C, Port Aransas, TX 78373-4125
- **Mission:** "To promote citizen interest and participation in the appreciation and preservation of Port Aransas' history, environment and ambiance." Full nonprofit mission: "to preserve and encourage the preservation, restoration and reproduction of historic buildings, structures, sites, places, objects, culture, ecology and customs of Port Aransas."
- **Properties managed:** Port Aransas Museum, Farley Boat Works (incl. Rick Pratt School of Wooden Boat Building), The Preserve at Farley Boat Works (under construction), Mercer Market (gift shop/retail), Chapel on the Dunes, Scow Schooner Project

### Key Founders

- **Dr. John Guthrie Ford (1944–2018)** — A founding member, retired psychology professor from Trinity University (taught 1972-1999). Authored several books on Port Aransas history, transcribed the Mercer Logs, wrote the "History Corner" column for the South Jetty, created multiple museum exhibits, conducted oral history interviews, and initiated historical marker installations. Co-authored *Images of America: Port Aransas* with Mark Creighton (Arcadia Publishing, Feb 28, 2011). Died April 10, 2018. PAPHA now awards the **Dr. John Guthrie Ford History Award** for historical scholarship.
- **Mark Creighton** — A founding and lifetime member. Cornell graduate, arrived in Port Aransas from the New Jersey shore in 1982. Museum archivist, local historian, columnist, and author. The primary force behind digitizing the photo archive (see Section 4). Co-author of *Images of America: Port Aransas*. First speaker in the 2024 Winter History Lecture Series.

## 2. Port Aransas Museum

### The Building — A 1910 Sears Kit House

- **Address:** 408 N. Alister Street / 101 E. Brundrett Street, Port Aransas, TX 78373 (same location, Community Center property)
- **Phone:** (361) 749-3800
- **Hours:** Thursday–Saturday, 1:00 PM–5:00 PM. Other times by arrangement.
- **Admission:** Free; docent-led tours available. Visit takes approximately 30-45 minutes.
- **Physical layout:** 2 rooms and a gift shop on the ground floor. Craftsman-style architecture.

**The building itself is a story:**
- Built from a **Sears, Roebuck and Company** mail-order catalog kit
- **Original builder:** Robert A. Mercer (son of Robert Ainsworth Mercer, the 1855 original settler of Mustang Island)
- **Brought to Port Aransas on a barge in 1910**
- Also known as the "Neblett house" — Municipal Court Judge Duncan Neblett and former Mayor Georgia Neblett lived there for 20 years
- **Survived the 1919 hurricane** and served as a temporary Coast Guard station after the existing station was destroyed
- Also survived Hurricane Celia (1970)
- **2006:** An out-of-town developer bought the property with plans to demolish it and build townhouses
- **2007:** Listed on **Preservation Texas's Most Endangered Places** list
- PAPHA acquired the house in 2006; original owners donated it to be moved
- Spent **$45,311 through August 1, 2008** to relocate and reconstruct it
- **April 2008:** House moved to current location on Community Center property
- City Council and City Manager helped find space on city property
- Port Aransas Garden Club provided landscaping
- **Museum opened December 2008** — Preservation Texas status: "Saved!"
- Only second director in history: Rick Pratt (2009-2018), then Cliff Strain (2018-present)

### Permanent Exhibits / Highlights

- **Fourth Order Fresnel Lens** — Originally installed in Lydia Ann Lighthouse from 1878 to 1952. Made in the 1860s by Augustin Henry Lapaute (whose ancestors were clockmakers for Louis XVI and Louis XVII). 2-foot-4-inch tall. Decommissioned by Coast Guard in 1954; city turned it over to museum after its 2008 opening. **November 2022:** Jean-Pierre Jacks — Lapaute's great-great-great-grandson — visited and identified a previously overlooked maker's engraving on the metal base.
- **Jetty train bell** — Bronze bell from the steam engine of the **Aransas Harbor Terminal Railway** (chartered June 13, 1892). The railroad transported granite blocks from Central Texas to the jetty construction site, a distance of 3.5 miles. The railway closed in 1947. Bell no longer has its clapper but visitors are encouraged to interact with it.
- **1920s/1930s films** — Rare film footage of Port Aransas, available for viewing and purchase at the museum
- **Oral histories** — Video interviews with important islanders; ongoing Oral Histories Project capturing stories of hurricanes, fishing, local characters
- **Genealogy scroll** — A paper scroll approximately **60 feet long** tracing about **20 local families** stemming from one 1854 marriage between **Franz Joseph Frandolig** and **Hannah Anna Ellen Schwander**. Families include the Moores, the Mathews, the Bujans, and ~17 others.
- **1940s Celia-damaged Farley boat** — A Farley boat wrecked by Hurricane Celia (1970), repaired remains on display
- **Gyotaku / fish prints** by **Dinah Bowman** — Learned gyotaku from Japanese masters in the late 1960s; work in the Smithsonian permanent collection; traveled with Smithsonian Traveling Exhibition Service through U.S., Canada, and Australia
- **Whale bones** — Large whale bone pieces including parts of a jaw and massive skull (reported by visitors)

### Rotating / Named Exhibits

- **"The Tarpon Era"** — Opened late February 2020. Chronicles 1880s–late 1950s. Items: tarpon rods/reels, Rodeo trophies, linen line-drying spool, silver creamer set from the Tarpon Club, Dinah Bowman tarpon fish prints, Aimee Semple McPherson's tarpon scale. Mounted tarpon from the first Deep Sea Roundup (1932) — believed caught by Totsy Millican (entered under husband North Millican's name).
- **"Taming the Channel"** — Covers capture and taming of Aransas Pass from Karankawa era through final jetty extension and harbor dredging in 1919. Spans three centuries.
- **"100 Years of Sport Fishing in Port Aransas"** — Past exhibit
- **"Historic Families and Characters"** — Opened **March 20, 2025**, runs through end of December 2025. Collaborative exhibit with direct input from families: Mary Jo Brundrett, Morgan Milligan, Keith Farley, Donald Belcher, Addie Belcher, Ned Teller, Bruce Baker, Dan Parker, Eva Rae Mercer Moreland (deceased). **Includes Port Aransas's Black residents "for the first time."** Focuses on "unsung heroes" and previously untold community stories. Brundrett family holdings include late 1800s photographs.
- **Surfing history exhibit** — History of surfing in Port Aransas from the early 1960s. ~20 surfboards from Port Aransas, Corpus Christi, Galveston, and South Padre Island shapers, plus classic boards, memorabilia, artwork, stories, photographs. Dan Parker (longtime surfer, former curator of Texas Surf Museum) presented slideshow. Parker and Michelle Christenson co-authored *Surfing Corpus Christi and Port Aransas* (Arcadia Publishing).
- **Hurricane/Harvey content** — Documents repeated community resilience through 1916 hurricane, Hurricane Celia (1970), Hurricane Harvey (2017)

### Gift Shop

Carries handcrafted items from local artisans, books by local historians, vintage photos (from the archive), caps, "Catch Some History" and "Farley Boat Works" t-shirts, postcards, note cards. Constantly changing stock of vintage Port Aransas photos.

## 3. The Photo Archive — MAJOR ASSET

### Scale

The archive has grown significantly over time:
- "In excess of 8,000 images" (early reporting, Creighton's initial digitization)
- "Over 25,500 archived photos and documents" (portaransas.org listing)
- "More than 38,000 photos and documents" (recent visitor guide article)
- "Close to 40,000 historical photos" (South Jetty, context of abandoned trailer discovery)

### Time Period & Subjects

Covers the late 1800s through the present. Subjects: fishing, storms, daily life, buildings, people, school children, the jetties, boats, the lighthouse, community events, families, the ferry, surfing history. Brundrett family holdings include late 1800s photographs.

### Mark Creighton's Role

He is the primary archivist who processes every historical photo added to the digital collection. He has been responsible for the digitization effort since the archive's creation. His digitization efforts created the foundation that has grown to 38,000-40,000 images. The vast majority of images in *Images of America: Port Aransas* come from his archive.

### Notable Discoveries

- **Abandoned trailer photos (~2020s):** Port Aransas resident Cathy Fulton found 12 photographs dated 1929-1931 in a drawer of an abandoned trailer near Beach Access Road 1. Envelope labeled "Port Aransas school children." Creighton noted the children may have been from "rag town," a tent community from the late 1920s-1930s.
- Archive contains oral histories, documents, and photographs from historic families including the Brundrett, Mercer, Farley, Moore, Mathews, and Bujan families.

### Digitization Status

Photos have been digitized into a "publicly accessible database." Copies can be purchased. **However:** no searchable online interface was found. Photos described as available "for viewing and purchase" — unclear if in-person only. No evidence of an online search portal.

## 4. Digital Presence Gap — MAJOR FINDING

### What PAPHA Has Physically

- **38,000-40,000** historical photographs and documents (archived, digitized internally)
- 1920s/1930s film footage
- Ongoing oral history recordings (audio/video interviews with islanders)
- 400+ antique fishing rods and reels (McKee Collection)
- 40+ boxes of vintage fishing tackle
- Extensive maritime artifact collection
- Two original Farley boats (Starfish and Tina)
- Vintage outboard motor collection
- Compass and binnacle collection
- 60-foot genealogy scroll
- Fourth Order Fresnel lens from 1860s
- Jetty train bell from Aransas Harbor Terminal Railway

### What Is Online

- **portaransasmuseum.org** — Basic WordPress/WooCommerce site. Has pages for exhibits, Farley Boat Works, events, membership. Limited content depth. No searchable photo database visible. No digital exhibits. No oral history audio/video accessible online.
- **Facebook pages** for Port Aransas Museum and Farley Boat Works — standard social media presence
- Archive described as "publicly accessible database" but no evidence of an online search interface

### The Gap

- **Almost none of the 38,000-40,000 photos appear to be accessible online**
- **No oral histories are published digitally** (no podcast, no YouTube, no audio archive)
- **1920s film footage not available online** in any form found
- **No virtual exhibits or digital storytelling**
- **No searchable online collection database**
- **The McKee Collection has a single page on the PAPHA site with no catalog or images**
- **The genealogy scroll is not digitized or viewable online**
- This represents a massive heritage content opportunity: a physical collection of extraordinary depth with essentially zero digital reach

## 7. The Preserve at Farley Boat Works

- **What it is:** A 6,000-square-foot maritime museum being constructed adjacent to the existing Farley Boat Works work sheds at 716 W. Avenue C
- **Architect:** David L. Walker of Corpus Christi (also designed the Port Aransas Art Center)
- **Design:** L-shaped structure; air-conditioned main exhibition space + unheated wing for boat display. Exterior of one section replicates the historic U.S. Lifesaving Station. Includes bathrooms, office, gift shop, event rental space.
- **PAPHA board approved architect:** September 2020
- **Ground broken:** May 2021
- **Funding:** FEMA grant — the original two storage structures/boat barns were destroyed by Hurricane Harvey (2017). FEMA program allowed combining funds from two demolished buildings to finance Phase One.
- **Construction status (as of February 2024):**
  - Shell and concrete slab completed; doors, windows, some electrical installed
  - Work had stalled for several months
  - **Mustang Homes of Port Aransas** hired in late 2023 for finish-out
  - Ashley Harris estimated opening in 6-12 months (i.e., August 2024–February 2025)
  - Delays attributed to FEMA bureaucracy and contractor availability
- **Current status (April 2026):** Unknown — no subsequent articles found confirming opening. **This is the single most important gap to verify with PAPHA.**

### Planned Collections for The Preserve

- **The McKee Collection:** "One of the most complete collections of offshore/inshore tackle on display anywhere." All rods wooden (split bamboo and cane), most restored with age-appropriate reels. 400+ antique fishing rods/reels, 40+ boxes vintage tackle. Potentially one of the largest public displays of antique tackle in the U.S. Currently displayed on two walls at the museum.
- Two original complete Farley boats: the **Starfish** and the **Tina**
- Vintage outboard motor collection
- Compass and binnacle collection
- Maritime maps and charts
- Elevated platform for public observation of boat restoration work
- Event rental space

## 8. Chapel on the Dunes

- **Location:** Atop a sand dune overlooking the Gulf, near intersection of 11th Street and Avenue B
- **Designation:** Oldest functional consecrated church on Mustang Island
- **Built:** 1937-1938 by **Aline B. Carter**, the "White Angel"

### Aline B. Carter
- Attended Wellesley College; harp instruction at Boston Conservatory; trained at Eric Pape School of Art
- Astronomy instructor, poet, artist
- Authored poetry collections: *Halo of Love* and *Doubt Not The Dream*
- **Texas Poet Laureate, 1947-1949**
- Called it "the Chapel of Eternal Light" — small windows frame morning Gulf light and sunset bay light
- Held Sunday school serving ice cream and cake to island children
- Died 1972

### The Chapel
- **Designer/Builder:** Ethel Wilson Harris, noted San Antonian artisan associated with Mission San Jose Pottery and WPA Arts and Crafts Division. Altar and tile work based on Aline's own drawings.
- **Interior Murals:** In 1972, artist John Patrick Cobb painted Biblical murals on whitewashed interior. Old Testament on north wall, New Testament on south wall.
- **Ownership:** Carter family retains ownership. Frank Carter (one of Aline's three sons) managed it until his death at age 99 in 2018.
- **Tours:** PAPHA offers free guided tours every first and third Saturday of each month, starting 9:15 AM, lasting 30-40 minutes
- **Weddings:** Available for rental ceremonies
- **Website:** chapelonthedunes.org

## 9. The Mercer Logs — Primary Source Documents

- **Origin:** Robert Ainsworth Mercer began the first entry **March 1, 1866**, using a ship's log format — third person, generally just facts, always concerned with the weather
- **Physical form:** Six large bound ledger books spanning 1866-1881
- **Authors:** Robert Mercer through his death (1875); sons John and Edward continued; evidence suggests wives occasionally contributed
- **Content:** Weather, coming and going of ships, life events, family life, feuds, storms, shipwrecks and rescues, education, place names, boat names, family names, parties, funerals, births
- **Civil War context:** Federal troops blockaded Aransas Pass 1862-1866, set fire to buildings, appropriated cattle. Mercers abandoned homes, relocated inland, returned 1866.
- **Preservation:** Five logs microfilmed by La Retama Library in Corpus Christi in 1976. A sixth log discovered in 1976 by a Mercer descendant in Rockport — awaits transcription. Dr. Ford transcribed logs 1-5 into searchable Microsoft Word documents.
- **Publication:** PAPHA published selections. Hardback copies sold at museum gift shop.

## 11. Coast Guard Station / Lifesaving Station History

| Year | Event |
|---|---|
| 1878 | First Aransas Life Saving Station established on Mustang Island at Cotter and Station Streets |
| 1915 | U.S. Life Saving Service merged into newly formed U.S. Coast Guard |
| 1916 | Original station destroyed by hurricane. The kit house (now the museum) served as temporary replacement |
| 1919 | Another devastating hurricane |
| 1925 | Second Life Saving / Coast Guard station opened; kit house sold to private sector |
| 1925-1976 | Second station in continuous service |
| 1976 | Second station replaced due to foundation erosion and increased workload |
| 2017 | Hurricane Harvey causes extensive damage to Coast Guard station |
| 2019 (April) | Harvey-damaged Coast Guard station demolished |

## 13. Historical Markers Program

PAPHA developed a local marker system using **8-inch by 8-inch ceramic tiles** designed by Wimberley-based Dunis Studios. Tiles feature the Lydia Ann Lighthouse, a rope symbol (for "Ropesville"), and a tarpon (for the name "Tarpon"). First marker announced at City Council meeting January 18, 2024.

- Initial 12 ceramic markers commissioned; future markers cast aluminum for coastal durability (~$300 each)
- Over 100 Port Aransas structures could potentially qualify
- Focus: buildings within Old Town boundary (established April 2024), oldest structures with minimal changes, particularly from 1920s-1940s
- Confirmed recipients include: Chapel on the Dunes, Cotter Avenue home (built 1927 by Leonard Brown Sr.)

## 15. Future Vision

### The Preserve Opening
- Single biggest near-term milestone. Last public report (Feb 2024): 6-12 months out. Current status unknown.

### Lydia Ann Schooner Project
- **60-foot** replica of a Texas Gulf Coast scow schooner (flat-bottomed cargo vessels, 1870s-1920s)
- Named for the Lydia Ann Channel
- Originated at Galveston and Trinity Bay Marine Museum (2001). Stalled after Hurricane Ike (2008).
- **2013**: Transferred to PAPHA
- **2015**: Construction resumed at Farley Boat Works. Dan Pecore leads the build team.
- Current: 57-foot hull, 13-foot breadth, 37-foot main hull and masts completed. Rudder unfinished, rigging not commenced.
- Survived Hurricane Harvey (2017) nearly unscathed
- When completed: **the only Texas scow schooner in existence anywhere**
- Vision: moored in city marina for historical tours through the jetties

### Expansion Trajectory
- PAPHA has grown from a small preservation society (founded 2002) to managing multiple properties with $2M+ in assets
- Pattern of steady acquisition: museum (2008), Farley Boat Works property (2011), The Preserve (ground broken 2021)
- Digital expansion is the obvious next frontier

## CONTENT HOOKS (Heritage Storytelling Angles)

1. **"A Sears Kit House Shipped on a Barge"** — The museum building is a 1910 Sears catalog house, brought on a barge, built by Mercer's son, survived the 1919 hurricane, served as Coast Guard station, almost demolished for condos in 2006, saved by PAPHA. That is a Texas story.

2. **"38,000 Photos Nobody Can See"** — The digital presence gap is the hook. A small-town museum sitting on one of the most significant coastal heritage photo archives in Texas, with 1920s film footage and oral histories — almost none accessible beyond the physical walls.

3. **"The Lens-Maker's Great-Great-Great-Grandson"** — In November 2022, Jean-Pierre Jacks visited from France and identified his ancestor Augustin Henry Lapaute's maker's engraving on the museum's Fresnel lens. A 160-year-old connection, rediscovered.

4. **"120 Boats and Counting"** — From Doyle Marek's high school shop class to the Rick Pratt School, an unbroken tradition of teaching ordinary people to build wooden boats. The Winter Texan volunteer culture is a human interest goldmine.

5. **"Totsy's Tarpon"** — The mounted tarpon from the first Deep Sea Roundup (1932) was entered under North Millican's name but believed caught by his wife Totsy. A women-in-fishing angle with nearly a century of history.

6. **"The McKee Collection Nobody Knows About"** — 400+ antique rods and reels, 40+ boxes of tackle, potentially one of the largest public displays in the U.S. — one webpage, no catalog, no images online.

7. **"60 Feet of Family"** — The genealogy scroll tracing 20 local families from a single 1854 marriage. The Moores, the Mathews, the Bujans — all one family.

8. **"For the First Time"** — The 2025 "Historic Families and Characters" exhibit includes Port Aransas's Black residents for the first time. A story about whose history gets told and who gets left out.

9. **"The Pirate's Poet's Chapel"** — Aline B. Carter, Texas Poet Laureate, Wellesley graduate, built a chapel on a sand dune in 1937 so she could watch the Gulf light. She held Sunday school with ice cream. It's still there.

10. **"The Train Bell That Built the Jetties"** — The Aransas Harbor Terminal Railway (chartered 1892) hauled granite from the Hill Country to build the jetties. The bell from the locomotive is at the museum. The railway closed in 1947. By 1990, only a few pilings remained.
