## Research compiled April 12, 2026

## 1. KARANKAWA & PRE-EUROPEAN HISTORY

### Archaeological Record
- **~5,000 BCE (5th millennium BCE):** Radiocarbon dating of artifacts places Karankawa groups on the Texas Gulf Coast as early as the fifth millennium BCE -- significantly earlier than the commonly cited ~2800 BCE figure
- Karankawa presence on barrier islands and mainland coast between Galveston Bay and Corpus Christi Bay is well-documented archaeologically
- Shell middens (mounds of discarded shells from harvested oysters, clams) are primary archaeological evidence of long-term habitation

### Karankawa Culture & Way of Life
- **Nomadic seasonal migration:** Moved between barrier islands (summer fishing camps) and mainland (winter hunting grounds)
- **Band structure:** Traveled in small bands of ~50 kinsmen led by a chief most of the year; bands converged into groups of 500+ individuals in winter when food was most plentiful
- **Material culture:** Crafted baskets and pottery lined with asphaltum (natural tar found on Gulf beaches); used longbows (described as nearly as tall as the archers themselves)
- **Body art:** Entire bodies covered in tattoos -- conveyed status, marital availability, acted as "passport" through territory. Bodies also painted. Europeans described them as "bizarre black marks"
- **Physical stature:** Archaeological studies show average height of 5'8" -- taller than most Europeans of the era, but NOT the mythical 7 feet tall claimed in colonial-era propaganda
- **Diet:** Fish, shellfish, deer, bison, roots, berries; seasonal dependence on different food sources drove migration patterns

### The Cannibalism Myth (Debunked)
- Karankawas practiced a *ceremonial* form of cannibalism to symbolically absorb power of foes -- ended by late 1600s
- No firsthand accounts of any form of cannibalism after the late 1600s
- Myths originated from failed Spanish conquistadors and missionaries who created propaganda to justify failed attacks
- Stephen F. Austin weaponized the myth: declared Karankawas "universal enemies to man" and called for "extermination"
- SMU historian Tim Seiter has led modern myth-busting research alongside Karankawa descendants

### Displacement & Genocide (1820s-1850s)
- **1820s:** Anglo-American colonists arrived under Stephen F. Austin's land grants -- settled directly in Karankawa territory
- **30,000+ settlers** arrived in and near Karankawa boundaries between 1820s-1830s
- Austin assigned a captain to "expel" the Karankawa from his land grant, leading to multiple attacks including the **Skull Creek massacre**
- Colonists used cannibalism propaganda to justify genocidal acts
- **1836:** Karankawa were fighting for survival by Texas independence
- **1858:** Following defeat by a force led by Juan Nepomuceno Cortina, the Karankawa were declared "extinct" -- a false designation
- Surviving Karankawas across the Gulf Coast retained and passed down culture generation after generation

### Modern Tribal Resurgence
- **Karankawa Kadla** ("mixed people") tribal organization formed ~2015 after individuals connected via social media
- Two clans: one centered in Corpus Christi, one in Galveston
- Now have a tribal council
- **November 2020:** Handbook of Texas changed its first sentence from "The now-extinct Karankawa Indians" to "The Karankawa Indians are an American Indian cultural group whose traditional homelands are located along Texas' Gulf Coast"
- Not yet recognized by state or federal government -- Texas recognition process is tangled with red tape
- **Indigenous Peoples of the Coastal Bend** nonprofit organized to protest industrial expansion near McGloin's Bluff (Corpus Christi Bay) where 40,000+ Karankawa artifacts have been excavated

### 2025 Donnel Point Archaeological Rediscovery
- **Location:** Donnel Point, along the La Quinta Channel on Corpus Christi Bay (NOT on Mustang Island, but in the broader Coastal Bend region)
- **Discovery:** Ingleside on the Bay resident Patrick Nye spotted a dense shell deposit in an eroded bluff while boating
- Local archaeologists confirmed it as an **archaic shell midden** matching historical accounts of Karankawa tribal camps dating back ~2,300 years
- Historically, four separate shell middens had been identified at Donnel Point in the 1930s
- Site is owned by the **Port of Corpus Christi**
- **Preservation fight:** Earthjustice lawyers representing Karankawa and Carrizo/Comecrudo Tribe of Texas asked U.S. Army Corps of Engineers to revoke an unused permit authorizing construction of an oil terminal at the site
- Donnel Point is among the last undisturbed tracts of land on nearly 70 miles of shoreline

## 2. EUROPEAN CONTACT & THE PIRATE ERA

### Spanish Exploration
- **1519:** Alonso Alvarez de Pineda -- first European to lay eyes on Mustang Island. Sailed through Aransas Pass; mapped a large bay he named Corpus Christi. Pineda was on a royal mission to map the unknown shoreline between Florida and Veracruz. His map is the **first known topographic rendering** of the pass-indented Texas coastline
- **1528:** Panfilo de Narvaez expedition; Alvar Nunez Cabeza de Vaca shipwrecked near Galveston Island. Cabeza de Vaca and companions later paddled across Aransas Pass in a canoe while trying to reach Mexico by following the coastline. NOTE: The claim that Cabeza de Vaca was ON Mustang Island is considered myth by historians -- he passed through the region
- **1800s:** Spanish brought horses ("mestenos") to the island -- the wild horses gave Mustang Island its name (originally called Wild Horse Island)

### Jean Lafitte & Pirate Activity
- **1817-1820:** Jean Lafitte headquartered his smuggling operation on Galveston Island (then part of Spanish Texas)
- **1820s:** Lafitte and his band of buccaneers spent significant time on Mustang Island and neighboring barrier islands
- Used Mustang Island as a camp and, according to legend, a place to hide treasure
- **Treasure legend:** Somewhere on the island is supposedly a Spanish dagger with a silver spike driven through the hilt, marking the spot where Lafitte buried a chest of gold and jewels
- Port Aransas became a favorite of pirates in early 19th century
- Lafitte's presence is deeply embedded in local lore -- the area's pirate heritage remains a major cultural touchstone

## 3. LYDIA ANN LIGHTHOUSE

### Authorization & Construction
- **March 3, 1851:** Congress authorized $12,500 for lighthouse construction at Aransas Pass
- Brick tower design selected over screwpile or cast-iron alternatives
- **Late December 1855:** Schooner carrying bricks for the tower foundered on the sandbar at Aransas Pass entrance during high seas. Crew rescued but ship and cargo were total loss
- **1856:** New bricks arrived, followed by lantern room, then a fourth-order Fresnel lens
- **Early 1857:** 55-foot octagonal tower and keeper's dwelling completed; tower painted brown
- **1857:** Light first illuminated -- used fourth-order Fresnel lens
- Second oldest lighthouse on the Texas coast; oldest surviving structure in the Aransas Pass/Corpus Christi area

### Civil War Destruction & Reconstruction
- **December 25, 1862:** Confederate General John B. Magruder ordered destruction of the tower
- Two kegs of powder exploded inside tower, damaging upper 20 feet of brickwork and destroying most of the circular staircase
- **1867:** Repairs began; lighthouse was **the last principal light along the coast to return to service** (spring 1867)

### Lydia Ann Channel Naming
- Origin is uncertain and debated
- Historical marker credits "the daughter of the first keeper" -- but that's inaccurate
- Keeper Frank Stephenson had a daughter named Lydia Ann, but he was the NINTH keeper, not the first
- Local historians believe it was more likely named after **Lydia Ann Dana Hastings Hull Wells**, wife of Texas Revolution veteran James B. Wells

- Butt hired a live-in caretaker (Rick Reichenbach, profiled by Texas Monthly) and oversaw restoration
- Grounds and tower remain **closed to the public** -- privately owned
- Only operating lighthouse in Texas with a live-in caretaker

## 4. ROBERT MERCER & EARLY SETTLEMENT

### The Mercer Family Timeline
- **Robert Ainsworth Mercer (1799-1875)**
- **1850:** Mercer established a sheep and cattle grazing station on nearby San Jose (St. Joseph) Island
- **1853:** Moved to San Jose Island without his family (who stayed in Alabama); established himself as a ship's pilot
- **1855:** Called for family to join him; built a cabin on Mustang Island at what he called **El Mar Rancho** -- relocated there with wife Agnes and children Mary Agnes, John, and Edward
- Mrs. Agnes Mercer is reputed to have been the **first white woman** in the area
- Mercer was appointed **Nueces County Wreck Master** -- oversight of disposition of vessels lost crossing Aransas Pass
- Family raised cattle and sheep, operated **Mercer Docks**, later opened the first general store
- **November 1880:** Sons William and Edward Mercer established a store on the island

### The Mercer Logs
- Exhaustive journals maintained from **1866 through 1877**
- Preserved by successive Mercer descendants
- Recognized as **primary historical documents** for the area
- Document daily life, weather, maritime traffic, and island events during the Reconstruction era

### Mercer Docks
- Major maritime infrastructure for early island settlement
- **1859:** First deep-draught steamship went through the Pass
- Regular steamship service ran between Mustang Island and New Orleans in the 1850s
- **1875:** Mercer Docks destroyed during the Hurricane of 1875, ending regular steamship service

## 5. JETTY CONSTRUCTION (1880s-1919)

### Phase 1: Federal Government (1880-1885)
- **1879:** Congress passed resolution authorizing deepening of Aransas Pass
- **May 1880:** Samuel M. Mansfield began work
- **1880-1885:** Built a 5,500-foot jetty of brush mattress covered with stone -- ultimately unsuccessful
- **1889:** U.S. government abandoned the project

### Phase 2: Private Sector (1890-1897)
- Local entrepreneurs formed the **Aransas Pass Harbor Company** to continue the project
- Jetty work commenced in 1890
- **1897:** Aransas Pass Harbor Co. went bankrupt and quit the project

### Phase 3: Army Corps of Engineers (1899-1919)
- **1899:** Congressional Rivers and Harbors Committee authorized return to Aransas Pass
- Army Corps focused first on completing the **north jetty**
- Construction used enormous **granite blocks** (many weighing several tons), quarried from the **Llano Uplift** region of Central Texas, transported via rail to the coast
- **D.M. Picton firm of Rockport** contracted for jetty work
- **1907:** North jetty nearing completion; construction of south jetty authorized
- **~1911:** Work considered substantially complete
- **1919:** Both north and south jetties fully completed

### Impact
- Created a reliable deep-water channel enabling both commercial shipping and recreational fishing
- Made Port Aransas accessible to larger vessels
- Directly enabled the tarpon fishing boom that gave the town its fame
- Eventually contributed to Port Aransas becoming the nation's 12th largest oil shipping port (1940s)

## 6. FERRY SYSTEM HISTORY

### Early Access (Pre-Ferry)
- Before ferry service, access to Mustang Island was primarily by boat
- **1920s:** Growing fame of Port Aransas fishing and beaches drove demand for automobile access

### The Railroad-Ferry System
- **1928:** Gail Borden Munsell initiated ferry service
- Munsell bought improvements of the Harbor Island Terminal Railroad
- Initially transported automobiles from Aransas Pass to Harbor Island on **railroad flat cars**, then by ferryboat to Port Aransas
- **The Mitzi:** First ferry boat, a side-wheel ferry that could carry **six automobiles**

### Development & Expansion
- **1931:** Munsell decked the railroad right-of-way and trestles, allowing cars to drive directly to the ferry landing
- **1931:** Roadway connected Aransas Pass to the ferry crossing
- **1931:** Causeways connected Flour Bluff to North Padre Island and North Padre to Mustang Island
- **1937:** Nine-car ferry added alongside the Mitzi
- **The Mitzi** eventually retired and replaced by the **Ruby**, the **Estelle**, and the **Nellie B**

### Route
- Originated in Aransas Pass, went to Harbor Island, then crossed the ship channel to Port Aransas

### Post-War Changes
- **1951:** Nueces County took over ferry operations; instituted a **$1 toll**
- **1951:** Melvin O. Littleton became superintendent (had moved to Port Aransas in 1937 as a fishing guide)
- **1954:** Road connecting Port Aransas with the Padre Island Causeway completed -- provided first direct vehicular access to the island from the south
- **1968:** TxDOT assumed ownership and operation of the ferries; **removed all tolls** -- ferries became free

### Current Operations
- TxDOT operates free ferry service 24/7
- Ferries cross the Corpus Christi Ship Channel between Harbor Island (Aransas Pass side) and Port Aransas
- Remains the primary vehicle access from the north; the only alternative is the long drive south via Padre Island

## 8. OIL INDUSTRY

- Port Aransas became the **nation's 12th largest oil shipping port** by the 1940s
- Oil shipped through the Aransas Pass channel, which had been deepened and stabilized by the jetty project
- **Oil depot** located just inside the Aransas Pass
- Vessels from the Port of Corpus Christi also transited Aransas Pass
- During WWII, this vital resource was protected against enemy attack by troops, dog patrols, and gun emplacements
- Oil economy operated alongside fishing as dual economic pillars through mid-20th century
- Oil tank breach during the **1919 hurricane** -- victims swept into the bay were covered in heavy crude oil, making identification impossible

## 9. WWII COASTAL DEFENSES

### Background
- **March 1941:** War Department created the **Southern Defense Command (SDC)** as part of national defense system, led by **Lt. Gen. Walter Krueger**
- SDC covered Southern US coastline from North Carolina to Brownsville
- **January 1942 (month after Pearl Harbor):** German U-boat reported just miles off the entrance to Aransas Pass
- Oil depot and Port of Corpus Christi vessels made Aransas Pass a perceived enemy target

### Military Response Timeline
- **January 1942:** SDC dispatched temporary field artillery battery of the **2nd Infantry Division** to Mustang Island; set up 105mm Howitzers
- **April 1942:** Battery E, **50th Coast Artillery Regiment** arrived
- Troops emplaced two **French-designed 155mm GPF guns** on **Panama mounts** (gun mount developed by U.S. Army in Panama during 1920s)
- Built reinforced concrete emplacements atop sand dunes, timber magazines, a commander station, searchlights
- Camp built for **360 men**
- Guns could fire 6-inch shells reaching targets up to **11 miles** away

### Operations
- Coastal defense lasted from **January 1942 to July 1944** (enemy naval threats ceased)
- **No weapons were ever discharged at any enemy**
- No actual conflicts with enemy craft occurred
- Population reportedly doubled from military personnel presence

### Historical Marker
- **World War II Coastal Defenses at the Aransas Pass** historical marker (Texas Historical Commission, Atlas #5507015267)
- Gun mount remnants still visible; local groups have worked to create a memorial

### Civil War Military Activity (Earlier)
- **April 1863:** Confederate artillery emplacement established on north Mustang Island -- named **Fort Semmes** (after Confederate Navy captain Raphael Semmes)
- **November 17, 1863:** Battle of Mustang Island -- Union forces under Brig. Gen. Thomas E.G. Ransom attacked Fort Semmes; Confederate garrison of <100 men under Major George O. Dunaway; USS Monongahela fired from offshore; Confederates surrendered
- Union troops occupied the island until **July 1864**
- **1846-1848:** Small fort built on Mustang Island during Mexican-American War to guard entrance to Aransas Bay; used until after Civil War

## 10. UT MARINE SCIENCE INSTITUTE (UTMSI)

### Origins
- **1935:** Dr. Elmer Lund, a UT Austin zoologist, traveled to Port Aransas to investigate a massive fish kill (now recognized as a red tide event)
- To study it, Lund erected a **one-room shack** on the old Corps of Engineers dock
- That building still exists, now called **"Dormitory B"** -- the oldest building on Mustang Island

### Founding & Early Years
- **1941:** Lund joined with **Dr. A.H. Wiebe** to build a small marine laboratory on the dock -- formally established as the **Institute of Marine Science**; Lund was first director
- Texas's **first permanent marine research station**
- **1945:** Lund established *Publications of the Institute of Marine Science* (later renamed *Contributions in Marine Science*, still published yearly)
- **1946:** UTMSI had taken root as the state's first permanent marine research station with a small but dedicated resident team

### Current Status
- Part of the University of Texas at Austin but located in Port Aransas
- Main campus: **855 E. Cotter Ave.**, on 72 acres of beachfront land at the mouth of Aransas Pass
- 15 faculty members, 35 graduate students
- Offers thesis-based MS and PhD in Marine Science
- Cutting-edge research, education, and outreach programs

### Harte Research Institute (Related but Separate)
- **September 19, 2000:** Edward H. Harte (former owner of Corpus Christi Caller-Times) donated **$46 million** to establish Harte Research Institute at Texas A&M University-Corpus Christi
- **Established 2001** -- dedicated solely to Gulf of Mexico research
- Located at TAMUCC, 6300 Ocean Drive, Corpus Christi -- NOT in Port Aransas
- Harte also championed creation of **Mustang Island State Park** near Port Aransas
- HRI and UTMSI are separate institutions (different universities) but both contribute to regional marine science; they collaborate on Gulf research

## 12. CINNAMON SHORE & MODERN DEVELOPMENT

### Development History
- **2007:** Cinnamon Shore opened as the first pedestrian-friendly, master-planned vacation home community in Port Aransas
- Developer: **Sea Oats Group** (Atlanta-based)
- Vision: Traditional seaside village modeled on New Urbanist design principles (often compared to Florida's Seaside/30A communities)
- Designed as environmentally friendly development

### Growth
- Grown from a few houses to **240+ homes, cottages, and penthouses**
- **Cinnamon Shore North:** Original 63-acre community, developed over 15+ years
- **Cinnamon Shore South:** Newer 147-acre Gulf-side expansion
- Some lot offerings sold out in **three days**
- COVID-19 pandemic accelerated demand for Cinnamon Shore South
- Real estate prices range from **mid-$300,000s to $2,500,000**

### Tension with "Old Port A"
- Port Aransas historically defined by its funky, laid-back fishing village character
- Cinnamon Shore represents a fundamentally different aesthetic: planned, manicured, upscale
- Development sits along Highway 361, technically between Port Aransas proper and Mustang Island State Park
- Broader concern: luxury resort development attracts wealthy second-home buyers while pricing out locals and workforce
- Hurricane Harvey accelerated this dynamic -- destroyed affordable workforce housing that was replaced by higher-end construction
- **NOTE:** Specific documented "tension" between old and new Port A is discussed more in local forums and anecdotal accounts than in formal journalism. The dynamic is real but not heavily covered in mainstream sources.

## 13. HURRICANE HARVEY (August 25, 2017)

### Pre-Landfall
- **August 24:** Mayor **Charles Bujan** issued evacuation order for Port Aransas (population ~4,000 at the time)
- Bujan realized "there was no place it could come except right here in Port Aransas"

### The Storm
- **Night of August 25, 2017:** Hurricane Harvey made landfall near Port Aransas and Rockport as a **Category 4 hurricane**
- Winds clocked at **130 mph**
- Harvey was the first Category 4 hurricane to make landfall in Texas since 1961

### Damage Assessment
- **100% of businesses damaged**
- **85% of homes damaged**
- City infrastructure alone (police station, library, other city buildings): **$50-70 million** in damage
- **$300 million** in property-tax value lost
- City laid off **10% of its workforce** due to revenue loss
- **1,200 people** left without homes or places to stay

### Recovery Timeline
- **August 28:** Bujan allowed residents back in (3 days after landfall) despite residents clamoring to return
- Most shops and businesses eventually reopened
- Six years later (2023), Port Aransas was still rebuilding

### Affordable Housing Crisis
- Old houses that were affordable for workers "mostly blew away or were so badly damaged that they were torn down"
- Workers forced to commute from Corpus Christi, Rockport, and other areas
- **October 2020:** Ground broke on affordable housing complex
- **Spring 2022:** 188-unit complex opened (80% low-income, 20% market rate) -- delayed from November 2021 due to labor/supply shortages
- Focus on higher-end redevelopment post-Harvey deepened the affordable housing crisis

### Other Major Hurricanes in Port Aransas History
- **1875:** Hurricane with 115 mph winds; destroyed Mercer Docks; 150 killed in Texas
- **1916:** August 18 hurricane; significant damage to Port Aransas
- **1919:** Center passed just south of Corpus Christi; 16-foot storm surge; 110 mph winds; **virtually wiped out the town** except for a few structures; oil tanks breached, victims covered in crude oil; death toll estimated 600-1,000 in the region
- Port Aransas earned the nickname **"Hurricane Junction"** (title of Cyril Matthew Kuehne's history book)

## 14. ADDITIONAL NOTABLE HISTORY

### Farley Boat Works
- **1910:** Brothers Barney and Fred Farley arrived on the Texas coast
- **1914:** Fred Farley moved to Port Aransas, established **Farley and Son Boat Works**
- **1915:** First Farley boat designed expressly for Port Aransas tarpon fishing
- Designed an 18-footer with low sides and high bow to handle choppy Gulf Coast waters
- Farley Boats set the standard along the Gulf Coast for fishing and sport from **1915 to mid-1970s**
- Now preserved as a historic site and expanding into a maritime museum

### Sea Turtle Export Industry
- At the turn of the 20th century, Port Aransas did big business in sea turtle export
- Some catches weighed up to 500 pounds
- Shipped live, on their backs, to market
- Industry eventually ended as conservation awareness grew

### Deep Sea Roundup (Fishing Tournament)
- **1932:** 25 charter and commercial fishermen calling themselves the **Boatmen Association** staged a three-day **Texas Tarpon Rodeo**
- North Millican caught the first winning tarpon (locals joke it was actually landed by his wife)
- **1934:** Dorothy Fair became the first woman champion
- Adopted the "Deep Sea Roundup" name as contest expanded beyond tarpon
- WWII hiatus; resumed after the war
- **1947:** Called the "Tarpon Rodeo and Deep Sea Roundup"
- **1965:** Became solely the "Deep Sea Roundup"
- **2020:** Paused for COVID-19 (only second pause after WWII)
- **Today:** Oldest fishing tournament on the Texas coast; sponsored by Port Aransas Boatmen Inc.

## KEY GAPS (Items Not Fully Verified)

1. **Ropesville name origin:** Why was the first post office called "Ropesville"? No source explains the derivation.
2. **Exact population figures 1900-1950:** Census data for these decades not found for Port Aransas specifically (unincorporated until 1911; very small settlement).
3. **WWII population doubling claim:** Multiple sources reference military personnel doubling the population, but no specific figures found for wartime population count.
4. **Oil industry specifics:** The "12th largest oil shipping port" claim is widely repeated but no primary federal source or ranking document was located. Specific annual tonnage figures not found.
5. **Cinnamon Shore community tension:** Documented tension between old Port A character and new luxury development is widely discussed anecdotally but lacks substantial formal journalism coverage.
6. **Donnel Point and Mustang Island connection:** The 2025 archaeological rediscovery is at Donnel Point on the La Quinta Channel (Corpus Christi Bay), NOT on Mustang Island. It's in the broader Coastal Bend but geographically distinct from Port Aransas.
7. **Lydia Ann Lighthouse National Register documentation:** Listed August 3, 1977, confirmed, but the actual NRHP nomination document was not accessed.
8. **Complete keeper list after 1921:** The lighthouse keeper list thins out after 1921 in available sources; the final Coast Guard keepers through 1952 deactivation are not fully documented online.
9. **Mercer Logs accessibility:** Referenced as primary documents but unclear where they are archived or if digitized.
10. **Port Aransas population 1940:** No data point found for this key WWII-era year.

## CONTENT HOOKS (Most Compelling Story Angles)

### 1. "They Said We Were Extinct"
The Karankawa story: from 5,000 years of Gulf Coast habitation, through deliberate genocide justified by propaganda, to being declared "extinct" in 1858, to the 2020 Handbook of Texas correction, to the 2025 Donnel Point fight. A story about erasure, survival, and reclamation that is actively unfolding right now.

### 2. The Lighthouse That Refused to Die
The Lydia Ann: authorized 1851, bricks lost in a shipwreck, completed 1857, blown up by Confederates on Christmas Day 1862, rebuilt, served nearly a century, deactivated 1952, bought by a grocery billionaire, relit 1988. The only Texas lighthouse still operating with a live-in keeper. And nobody can agree on who "Lydia Ann" actually was.

### 3. A Town That Has Been Destroyed and Rebuilt Multiple Times
Port Aransas has been leveled by the 1875 hurricane (Mercer Docks gone), the 1919 hurricane (town virtually erased, population dropped to 50), and Hurricane Harvey in 2017 (100% of businesses damaged). Each time it came back. "Hurricane Junction" is not just a nickname -- it's an identity.

### 4. Three Names, One Identity
Ropesville (1888) to Tarpon (1896) to Port Aransas (1911) -- a town that renamed itself twice in 23 years, each time reflecting what it wanted to become. The fishing village that became a port that became a resort.

### 5. The Pirate's Dagger
Lafitte's buried treasure, marked by a Spanish dagger with a silver spike through the hilt. Unverified, unfound, undeniable as a story. Perfect hook for the pirate-era chapter.

### 6. The Red Tide That Built a University
In 1935, a massive fish kill drew one zoologist to Port Aransas. He built a one-room shack. That shack became UTMSI -- Texas's first permanent marine lab. The building still stands as the oldest structure on Mustang Island.

### 7. The Affordable Housing Paradox
Harvey destroyed the affordable housing stock. What got rebuilt was upscale. The people who make Port Aransas work -- the fishing guides, restaurant workers, ferry operators -- can't afford to live there anymore. Cinnamon Shore lots sell out in three days. An 188-unit affordable complex is a band-aid on a structural wound.

### 8. Granite From the Hill Country to the Gulf
The jetty stones -- enormous granite blocks quarried from the Llano Uplift in Central Texas and hauled by rail to the coast. Three attempts over 39 years (1880-1919) to tame one pass. The jetties literally created modern Port Aransas.

### 9. Christmas Day Destruction
December 25, 1862: Confederate General Magruder orders two kegs of powder detonated inside the Lydia Ann lighthouse. The upper 20 feet of brick are destroyed. It would be the last principal light on the Texas coast restored after the war.

### 10. The Mercer Logs
A family's 11 years of daily journals (1866-1877) on a barrier island during Reconstruction. The primary source documents for understanding what life was actually like in this place. Where are they now? Can they be digitized? A preservation story within the heritage story.
