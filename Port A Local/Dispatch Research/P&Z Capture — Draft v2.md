# How Port Aransas Decides

*Five governance bodies. The same households. What the records show — and what the disclosure forms cannot.*

---

## The lede

On the 413-page agenda packet for the January 17, 2019 Port Aransas City Council meeting, the most consequential decision of the night fits in seven words. They appear at page 71, in the commentary line for Item 7-H — the contract awarding the city's hotel-and-conference center procurement:

> *INFORMATION SENT SEPARATE BY CITY MANAGER FOR REVIEW.*

That is the entire substantive content, in the public meeting packet, of the deliberation that resulted in a 7-0 council vote awarding the project. The grading rubric the City has since confirmed was used to evaluate the two bidders is not in the packet. A full-text search of the 413 pages returns zero hits for the words "rubric," "175," "182," "71.5," or "74%." The resolution template in the packet lists the wrong opponent — McCombs Properties rather than Palmilla Beach — and leaves the awardee field blank.

The Council voted 7-0 anyway.

The grading rubric the City has since produced under a Texas Public Information Act request shows that the bidder receiving 7-0 votes — Cinnamon Shore — scored **175 of 245 possible points (71.5 percent)**. The competing bidder — Palmilla Beach — scored **182 of 245 (74 percent)**. The category the lower-scoring bidder won most decisively was *"minimal impact of future tax revenue to the City."*

The seven words on page 71 of the packet describe a decision that wasn't, in any documentary sense, made in the public meeting. The decision was made elsewhere. Where, and by whom, is what the records — when assembled across multiple sources — tell us.

---

## What the records say

Port Aransas is governed by five named bodies that the city's organizational documents present as independent of each other:

- **City Council** — seven elected members; primary legislative + procurement-vote authority
- **Planning & Zoning Commission** — seven members appointed by Council; recommends zoning + development approvals
- **Charter Review Commission** — five-member volunteer board, statutorily six-month terms; recommends amendments to the city charter
- **Tourism Bureau Board of Directors** — sixteen voting members + ex-officio; allocates the city's Hotel Occupancy Tax pass-through, which totaled approximately $10.5 million in 2023
- **Port of Corpus Christi Authority** — regional commission with jurisdiction over Harbor Island and the ship channel adjacent to Port Aransas

The city's organizational chart is not wrong. These bodies are technically separate. They have distinct appointment processes, distinct meeting cycles, and distinct statutory jurisdictions.

In practice, looked at through the records that span all five, they are not separate.

---

## The same three seats

The Charter Review Commission is, by Port Aransas city statute, a five-member volunteer board appointed for six-month terms. The council appoints five citizens at its first regular meeting in July of every fourth year.

According to the Commission listing produced by the City Secretary in May 2026, **three of its five seats have been held by the same residents continuously since October 15, 2020**. One seat is currently vacant. The fourth current member was appointed October 16, 2025. Of the four named members, three have completed Texas Open Meetings Act training; one has not.

Five-plus years on a six-month seat. The body that drafts the city's charter — the document that defines how the city governs itself — has had its composition functionally locked since the previous procurement cycle. The next opportunity to change anything in the charter must be initiated through the same body whose composition the proposed change might address.

That is one body, three seats, five years. The pattern repeats across the others.

## The bodies that decide, by household

What the records show, when the membership of all five bodies is laid alongside published deeds, business filings, real estate licenses, building permits, and the city's own roster pages, is that **the seats themselves do not rotate as the city's organizational chart implies. The households do.**

The records are not new. A single July 2, 2009 Port Aransas South Jetty market roundup names four of the principals described in the paragraphs below — three on the residential-development side, one as a broker — as operators of adjacent projects on the same Port Aransas corridor. The architecture this piece describes is at minimum seventeen years old in the news-of-record.

The piece does not name individuals beyond what corporate filings and on-record sources require. Each paragraph below describes a household by the touchpoints that household holds across the five bodies. Readers who live in Port Aransas will recognize each household; readers who do not will absorb the architecture.

### Household A

Across four of the bodies that decide what happens to Port Aransas — Charter Review, Port of Corpus Christi Authority, Tourism Bureau Board, and City Council — four members of one extended household sit or recently sat. The senior member of the household has held a Charter Review seat continuously since 2020 and twelve years on the Port Authority, eight of those as Chair. Harbor Drive on the city's port-side approach was renamed in his honor on the Commission's vote. His daughter currently holds the Retail seat on the Tourism Bureau Board. Her husband was elected to City Council Place 1 in May 2023 and chose not to seek a second term in 2025. Public deed records show the household holds four parcels of Port Aransas residential and commercial real estate totaling roughly $3.4 million in 2026 appraised value, including a platted subdivision the County records under the household's family name.

### Household B

Two of the seats on the city's Planning & Zoning Commission and City Council are held by members of the same family. The Council seat is currently held by the body's Mayor Pro-Tem. The same household includes the spouse of the Council seat-holder, who is co-owner of a multi-restaurant operation that includes Tortuga's Saltwater Grill, FINS Grill and Icehouse, MacDaddy's Family Kitchen, and Deep Sea Headquarters; a member of the next generation of the family is the Director of Business Leadership and Development for a Port Aransas tourism-marketing operation. The Tourism Bureau Board seat held in the Bar/Restaurant category is filled by an employee who serves as Director of Operations for the household's restaurant entities. Public records show three parcels under household-name ownership totaling roughly $3.2 million in 2026 appraised value.

### Household C

One of the three Charter Review Commission members held continuously since October 2020 holds Texas Broker Associate credentials at a major international real estate brokerage and has, by self-disclosure on her brokerage's professional site, "more than 30 years of experience in real estate, building, and development." Public Texas Secretary of State filings show two LLCs registered in Port Aransas under her spouse — one formed in 2006 and still in existence, one formed in 2014 and tax-forfeited in 2021. Public construction-permit databases list 137 permitted projects under those entities. Coverage in the Port Aransas South Jetty attributes to her spouse's custom-build operation a planned development of seventy to ninety "beach bungalow style" houses on pilings adjacent to the Dune Crest Condominiums on State Highway 361 — "which will be short-term rentals." A separate Texas LLC associated with the household's investor network owns 49 parcels in Port Aransas, including approximately twelve acres of raw beach land adjacent to the planned bungalow development; the 49 parcels carry a 2026 appraised value of more than $5.1 million in the partial parcel set retrieved.

The body that recommends approvals on permits of that scale is the city's Planning & Zoning Commission. Her spouse has held a seat on it.

### Household D

Another of the Tourism Bureau Board's voting seats is held in a household where the spouse operates Brandon Lafayette Homes LLC — a custom-build company published on Palmilla Beach's "Approved Expert Builders" list. The Bureau Board seat-holder herself is the household's spouse, in the Professional Services category of the Bureau's roster. The household's primary residence carries a $2.3 million 2026 appraised value; public records also show a platted subdivision under the household's family name.

The "Approved Expert Builders" designation is published on the Palmilla Beach development's own marketing site. Palmilla Beach was the losing bidder in the 2019 hotel-and-conference center procurement described above. The household's custom-build operation is, on the developer's own published page, approved to build inside that development.

### And the pattern across the Bureau

The pattern is consistent across the bodies. Three of the local-resident-held seats — two on the Tourism Bureau Board and one on the Charter Review Commission — are filled by individuals whose spouses operate in the construction, brokerage, or development tier the bodies' decisions affect. Two of the Bureau's voting seats are held by principals of two of Port Aransas's largest residential-real-estate brokerages. Multiple seats are held by employees or principals of property-management operations managing hundreds of vacation-rental units in the city, including national-platform franchises (Avantstay, Sway, Portoro). One seat is held by an operator whose primary residence is in Corpus Christi, not Port Aransas.

This is not a finding about any one individual's compliance with anything. It is a description of the seat-by-seat structure of the bodies that decide.

## The Tourism Bureau

The body whose composition the previous section's pattern most heavily concentrates is the same body that allocates the city's $10.5 million Hotel Occupancy Tax pass-through. Six of the Tourism Bureau's roster, on the page that the bureau itself publishes, are members of the network the records describe.

The current Chair is a real estate broker who previously served six years as Mayor and ten years as a City Council member, and who sat on the 2019 Hotel and Conference Center grading committee whose rubric did not appear in the public meeting packet. **He co-owns a Texas-registered LLC formed in May 2016 — within months of his terming out as Mayor — with two other named members.** One of those co-members served as Tourism Bureau Chair preceding the current Chair's tenure and **personally led the search committee that selected the Bureau's most recent prior CEO in January 2017.**

Two consecutive Tourism Bureau Chairs have therefore been members of the same Texas LLC. The Chair preceding both selected the CEO who served between them. All three are real estate brokers operating on the same island.

The Bureau Board also seats:

- The General Manager of Vacation Rentals at the development that won the 2019 conference-center procurement
- The principal of the development that lost the 2019 conference-center procurement
- The wife of the household described in the Charter Review section above, on the Retail seat
- An employee of the multi-restaurant household described in the Council/P&Z section above, on the Bar/Restaurant seat
- A representative of one of the national corporate short-term-rental management platforms now operating at scale in Port Aransas — Sway, since acquired by Avantstay
- The city's career City Manager, ex-officio

The career City Manager has held the post across multiple council compositions, sat on the 2019 grading committee, and remains the executor of HOT-funded contracts the Bureau directs.

The Bureau is, on its public roster, the social ledger of how Port Aransas decides. It does not need to be opaque. It is published.

## What the disclosure forms cannot see

The piece would, in another city, have at this point reported on the conflict-of-interest filings that Texas Local Government Code Chapters 171 and 176 require local government officers and the vendors they transact with to maintain.

Port A Local filed a Texas Public Information Act request asking the City of Port Aransas whether any Form CIQ — the vendor disclosure required when a vendor's relationship with a sitting officer crosses statutory thresholds — was on file from 2017 through 2026 naming Sea Oats Group LLC, ZJZ Hospitality LLC, Cinnamon Shore (or its General Partner), or any of their named principals.

The City Secretary's response, in writing: *"The City of Port Aransas has identified no records responsive to this portion of your request. This portion of your request is now closed."*

Zero records. Nine years. The Form CIQ regime is a vendor-side disclosure with a § 176.006 trigger that may not have fired in any of the relationships above. The form's design captures vendor relationships with named officers; the form does not capture the kind of household-network overlap this piece describes.

The officer-side equivalent — Form CIS filings under § 176.003, and Chapter 171.004 substantial-interest abstention affidavits — is the subject of a focused follow-up records request now pending with the City Secretary.

The architecture the records describe is not, in itself, a Chapter 171 or Chapter 176 violation. It is a description of an architecture that those forms were not designed to surface.

## What gets built when this is who decides

The 2019 Hotel and Conference Center procurement awarded a 7-0 vote to a project located inside Cinnamon Shore II. Public meeting records confirm that on July 16, 2020 — eighteen months later — the same City Council voted 7-0 to relocate the project from Cinnamon Shore II to the Brookdale parcel two miles north on State Highway 361, under a different ownership entity ("PA Waterfront, LP / The Brookdale Group, LLC"). On November 19, 2020, the Council passed Resolution 2020-R59 extending the Development Agreement with a third entity, "The Inn, Spa & Conference Center, LP," through December 31, 2022. The developer's filed timeline projected closing on land February 2021, PUD amendment August 2021, infrastructure design December 2021, and groundbreaking December 2022.

As of May 2026, no hotel-and-conference center has been constructed on the parcel originally awarded the contract or on the Brookdale parcel.

The pattern of demolition-then-development-without-completion is not isolated to the conference-center procurement. In July 2022, Fisherman's Wharf Holding LLC — operated by a current Tourism Bureau Board member whose primary residence is in Corpus Christi — demolished a longtime Port Aransas community institution at 136 W Cotter to construct The Aransas Club, a 26-unit private condominium development. As of mid-2025, the project's marketing language described its inventory as "last remaining harborfront properties" — that is, still selling. Two prominent post-Harvey Port Aransas real-estate-development decisions have, between them, produced one demolished community asset and one slow-selling private condominium tower, and zero completed hotel-and-conference centers.

## The pattern, in motion

The architecture is not dormant. It is currently producing decisions.

In its 89th legislative session in 2025, the State of Texas passed House Bill 2844, which created Texas Health & Safety Code Chapter 437B and takes effect July 1, 2026. The state law preempts local food-truck health-and-sanitation permitting, caps the state license at $150 plus inspection, and leaves municipalities authority only over zoning, location, parking, fire, and noise. A companion measure, Senate Bill 1008, capping city food-establishment fees at the DSHS schedule, took effect September 1, 2025.

On April 21, 2026, the Port Aransas City Council voted approval of first readings on three ordinance drafts that occupy every avenue Section 437B.101 preserved: a new Section 12-45 governing "Mobile Food Vending"; a Chapter 25 zoning amendment; and a Chapter 10 noise amendment. The drafts include a new annual city site/location authorization, district-by-district zoning gates, a multi-vendor "Food Truck Park" classification, parking and waste and restroom standards, and noise enforcement. The drafts were prepared in Planning & Zoning workshops and are formally recommended by P&Z to Council. Staff memos from the city's Development Services Director, in the public meeting packet, describe the ordinance as *"structured to avoid conflicts with state preemption."*

The state's law takes effect July 1, 2026. The city is moving to lock its gate before that date arrives.

The structural pattern — Planning & Zoning prepares; Council adopts; the bodies that decide preserve the maximum scope the state has not yet preempted — is the same pattern the rest of this piece has described. The conference-center procurement was a single large decision that flowed through it. The food-truck ordinance is a small one. The architecture is the same.

---

## The fix path is the same body

The Charter Review Commission described in the second section of this piece — three of five seats held by the same residents since October 2020, one vacant, one without current Texas Open Meetings Act training — is the body the city's charter requires to be seated to recommend any amendment to that charter. The next regular cycle for the Commission's full reseating, by city statute, is July 2029, unless an extraordinary act of the same Council whose composition the records describe seats one earlier.

The architecture is, in this sense, internally consistent. The body that would produce a recommendation to change the architecture is the body whose composition the architecture has produced.

---

## Close

Leadership decides what happens to a town — not culture or destiny.

The decisions the records describe are decisions, not weather. The bodies that produced them are documented. The households that fill those bodies are documented. The pattern across the bodies, when assembled from records that exist and have always existed, is documented.

The records describe an architecture. The architecture was decided. The next set of decisions is still open.
