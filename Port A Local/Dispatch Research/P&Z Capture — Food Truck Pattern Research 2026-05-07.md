## 1. Texas state-level food-truck permit law (HB 2844 / Ch. 437B)

🟢 **House Bill 2844, 89th Texas Legislature (2025).** Creates a new chapter, **Texas Health & Safety Code Chapter 437B**, devoted entirely to the licensing and inspection of mobile food vendors statewide. Signed by Gov. Abbott June 20, 2025.
- **Effective date for licensing scheme:** **July 1, 2026** (Sec. 437B.004 effective Sept. 1, 2025).
- Source: bill text & analysis at [capitol.texas.gov — HB 2844 analysis](https://capitol.texas.gov/tlodocs/89R/analysis/html/HB02844E.htm); Texas Tribune coverage at [texastribune.org — Texas to launch a statewide food truck permit on July 1](https://www.texastribune.org/2026/04/06/texas-food-truck-statewide-licensing-fee/); Houston Public Media [houstonpublicmedia.org](https://www.houstonpublicmedia.org/articles/arts-culture/food/2026/04/07/548340/texas-food-truck-statewide-licensing-fee/); KIII-TV [kiiitv.com](https://www.kiiitv.com/article/news/local/texas-food-truck-permit-law-aims-to-streamline-statewide-operations-for-mobile-vendors/503-ec4252d8-7fc2-445c-8b67-f265d36f9f0a).

🟢 **Preemption language (Sec. 437B.003):** Chapter "preempts a local authority's power to prohibit or regulate mobile food vendors in a manner that conflicts with this chapter." DSHS (Texas Department of State Health Services) administers the license; local jurisdictions can no longer issue duplicative health/sanitation permits for mobile food units once the chapter takes effect. License fee capped at $150 plus inspection costs (Sec. 437B.058).

🟢 **What is *preserved* for cities (Sec. 437B.101):** vendors must "comply with all state and local laws in the jurisdiction in which the mobile food vendor operates, including all fire codes, **location restrictions, and zoning codes**." Section 437B.002 lets cities maintain regulations *provided they don't conflict with the chapter*.

🟢 **Companion statute — SB 1008, 89th Leg. (2025), eff. Sept. 1, 2025.** Limits city authority to require duplicative permits or charge fees that exceed DSHS's own. Annual local fees capped at the DSHS schedule. Cities that retain inspection roles must give 60 days' notice to stakeholders before changing fees, permits, or inspection protocols.
- Source: [capitol.texas.gov — SB 1008 analysis](https://capitol.texas.gov/tlodocs/89R/analysis/html/SB01008I.htm); [texaspolicyresearch.com — SB 1008 89R](https://www.texaspolicyresearch.com/bills/89th-legislature-sb-1008/); DSHS local-ordinance registry [dshs.texas.gov public-notice s.b-1008](https://www.dshs.texas.gov/sites/default/files/foodestablishments/pdf/Local%20Ordinance%20Registry/public-notice-s.b-1008-002.pdf).

**Net effect:** As of July 1, 2026, a Texas city cannot bar food trucks through health-department permitting or charge a stand-alone local food-safety fee. The legal pathway left to a city is **zoning, location, parking, fire-code, and noise**.

## 2. Port Aransas food-truck ordinance development (the load-bearing record)

🟢 **City Council Regular Meeting, Tuesday, April 21, 2026.** Council voted to approve **first readings** of *three* ordinance amendments designed expressly "in response to House Bill 2844." The three drafts are routed to layer **on top of** the state permit, using the avenues 437B.101 leaves open.
- Source: official agenda PDF, City of Port Aransas civicweb portal, Meeting ID 9673 — [cityofportaransas.civicweb.net/document/167876/](https://cityofportaransas.civicweb.net/document/167876/).
- Coverage: [portasouthjetty.com — "Food truck regs get initial OK"](https://www.portasouthjetty.com/articles/food-truck-regs-get-initial-ok/) (April 23, 2026).

**The three ordinance items, verbatim from the agenda (items 8.C / 8.D / 8.E):**

> **8.C — ORDINANCE - 1st Reading** Amending the Code of Ordinances Chapter 12 "Licenses and Business Regulations", Article III "Food Establishments" By Adding Section 12-45 "Mobile Food Vending" To Establish Regulations Specific to Mobile Food Vendors, Including Operational Categories, Site Standards, Parking, Waste Management, Restroom Availability, And Noise Limitations… *(Agenda Item Report AIR-26-057)*
>
> **8.D — ORDINANCE - 1st Reading** Amending the Code of Ordinances Chapter 25 "Zoning"… To Provide for Mobile Food Vendor Uses, Including Zoning Districts Allowances and Parking Requirements… *(AIR-26-056)*
>
> **8.E — ORDINANCE - 1st Reading** Amending the Code of Ordinances Chapter 10 "Health and Sanitation", Article IV "Noise"… *(AIR-26-058)*

🟢 **Staff statement on intent (AIR-26-057 / AIR-26-056 background, identical language):**

> "Staff has been working with the Planning and Zoning Commission to develop a comprehensive update to the City's operational regulations for mobile food vendors **in response to House Bill 2844**. The proposed ordinance is intended to align with state law while maintaining reasonable local standards…The ordinance is structured to **avoid conflicts with state preemption**, particularly related to permitting, operations, and mobility requirements."
> — Nicole Boyer, Development Services Director, City of Port Aransas. Agenda packet, pp. 31, 39.

🟢 **What the city is keeping for itself, by section of the draft:**
- **Sec. 12-45.5 Authorization Required:** "No mobile food vendor shall operate within the City without first obtaining an annual **site/location authorization** from the Development Services Department." (Not a health permit; an additional city authorization layered on top of the state license.)
- **Sec. 12-45.7 Operational Categories:** Three categories — primary use on private property; secondary use on improved private property; **multi-vendor sites** ("commonly referred to as a Food Truck Park") that are a "distinct use and require separate city administrative authorization."
- **Sec. 12-45.9 Location:** "Mobile food vendors may operate only in zoning districts where such use is permitted pursuant to Chapter 25" — i.e., the city retains the gate by deciding *which districts permit the use*.
- **Chapter 25 amendments (AIR-26-056):** "aligns mobile food vendor allowances with zoning districts where restaurant uses are currently permitted." Mobile-food vending is being grafted into the existing zoning categories (T, R-1, R-2, TR-1/2/3, C-1, C-2, I-1, HI, CZ-1, CZ-2, CPOZ) with city-determined allowances and parking standards.
- **Chapter 10 noise amendments (AIR-26-058):** generators, amplified sound, and after-hours operation become enforceable through the city's existing noise framework.

🟢 **Procedural status:** First readings only (April 21, 2026). Texas home-rule cities and Port Aransas's charter require multiple readings before adoption; the South Jetty notes "the proposed regulations still must get approvals at two more meetings to become law." HB 2844 takes effect **July 1, 2026** — the city is in a roughly ten-week window between first reading and state effective date.

🟢 **Genealogy — P&Z is the recommending body.** Both AIR-26-056 and AIR-26-057 state: "The Planning and Zoning Commission held workshops and is bringing this draft forward as their formal recommendation." This is the architectural fingerprint the section uses: P&Z (an appointed commission, no recall procedure, no public-vote mechanism) drafts the rules; council adopts; the rules then govern a category of business that several officials' own businesses compete with.

## 3. Historical Port Aransas gating of food trucks

🟢 **2014 ordinance + petition repeal.** Port Aransas City Council passed an ordinance permitting a small percentage of food trucks **on the beach**; residents filed a petition, the measure went to voters, and was rejected.
- Source: [portasouthjetty.com — "Permit process controls beach gatherings"](https://www.portasouthjetty.com/articles/permit-process-controls-beach-gatherings/) (July 31, 2024). City Manager David Parsons quoted: "Council approved that ordinance, but then the citizens filed a petition to kill it, and it went to the voters, and it got trounced."

🟢 **1994 Coastal Management Plan ban on commercial activity on the beach** — referenced in the same article as the longstanding source of authority for the city's beach-vending prohibition. Full text of the 1994 plan not located in this research pass.

🟡 **Existing code through April 2026:** No code section permits free-roaming mobile food vendors as a category. (Municode is a JS-rendered library and could not be machine-extracted in this pass; the staff report's premise — that this is a *new* Section 12-45 being *added* to Chapter 12, Article III — is itself the documentary evidence that no city-level mobile-food-vending framework currently exists in the code. Restaurants can sell from a food truck "permanently parked on the property"; otherwise the activity is not provided for. Source: tourism-industry summary at [thedunesporta.com](https://thedunesporta.com/blog/top-food-trucks-to-try-in-port-aransas/), corroborated by absence of a Sec. 12-45 in current Chapter 12.) ## 4. Model codesets and best-practices references

🟢 **American Planning Association (APA) — Planning Advisory Service.** APA publishes both a long-form PAS report ("Regulating Food Trucks") and a shorter PAS QuickNotes 49 ("Planning for Food Trucks") that summarize four key policy considerations municipalities use to update business-licensing and development regulations for mobile food vendors. APA's PAS reports are the canonical municipal-planning reference library; subscriptions span hundreds of city/county planning departments nationally.
- Sources: [planning.org — PAS Reports](https://www.planning.org/pas/reports/); [planning.org — Planning for Food Trucks (PAS QuickNotes 49)](https://www.planning.org/publications/document/9007644/); APA-hosted "Regulating Food Trucks" reference PDF surfaces at [planning-org-uploaded-media.s3.amazonaws.com](https://planning-org-uploaded-media.s3.amazonaws.com/document/open-EIP36.pdf).

🟢 **Institute for Justice — *Food-Truck Freedom: How to Build Better Food-Truck Laws in Your City*.** Recommendation framework derived from cities (Los Angeles, Austin, New York) with mature, non-protectionist mobile-vendor codes. Two stated principles: "(1) no protectionism; and (2) clear, narrowly tailored, and outcome-based laws." IJ has also published *Food Truck Truth* and *Street Eats, Safe Eats*, which use county-level census data across 12 years to find that growth in food trucks does not reduce restaurant counts.
- Sources: [ij.org — Food-Truck Freedom](https://ij.org/report/food-truck-freedom/); [ij.org — Food Truck Truth](https://ij.org/report/food-truck-truth/); [ij.org — Mobile Food Vendor Freedom Municipal Ordinance (model ordinance)](https://ij.org/legislation/mobile-food-vendor-freedom-ordinance/); APA cross-listing at [planning.org — Food Truck Freedom resource](https://www.planning.org/knowledgebase/resource/9139442/).

🟢 **Texas Municipal League (TML)** — has published guidance on the SB 1008 / HB 2844 changes for member cities, including the August 15, 2025 update at [tml.org — August 15, 2025, Number 31](https://www.tml.org/1063/August-15-2025-Number-31). TML is the standard reference body Texas cities use when adapting to state preemption.

🟡 **Comparable Texas city codesets in operation.** Houston, Austin, Fort Worth, Mansfield, Cibolo, Fredericksburg, and League City have published mobile-food-vendor permitting frameworks already pre-dating HB 2844 — the operational precedents the city could have adopted by reference rather than drafting *de novo*.
- Sources: [houstonconsumer.org — Mobile Food Units (Houston Health)](https://www.houstonconsumer.org/services/permits/food-permits/mobile-food-units); [mansfieldtexas.gov — Mobile Food Vendor](https://www.mansfieldtexas.gov/1954/Mobile-Food-Vendor); [cibolotx.gov — Mobile Food Vendor Permitting Process](https://www.cibolotx.gov/business/building_permits___inspections/mobile_food_vendor_permitting_process.php); [fbgtx.org — Mobile Food Unit Permits](https://www.fbgtx.org/1253/Mobile-Food-Unit-Permits); [leaguecitytx.gov — Permits and Ordinances](https://www.leaguecitytx.gov/4567/Permits-and-Ordinances).

## Closing report (≤400 words)

**The three highest-leverage findings, ready for the Dispatch section:**

1. **The state forced an opening; the city is layering its own gate.** Texas HB 2844 (89th Leg., 2025) creates Health & Safety Code Ch. 437B, takes effect **July 1, 2026**, preempts local food-truck *health/sanitation* permits, and caps the state license at $150 + inspection cost. Section 437B.101 leaves cities **only** zoning, location, parking, fire, and noise. Companion SB 1008 (eff. Sept. 1, 2025) caps city food-establishment fees at the DSHS schedule. The legal pathway left to gate food trucks is exactly the pathway Port Aransas's three April 21 drafts run down.

2. **The April 21, 2026 first-reading vote is the load-bearing record.** Council approved first readings of three ordinance drafts — Chapter 12 (new Sec. 12-45 "Mobile Food Vending"), Chapter 25 ("Zoning") amendments, and Chapter 10 (noise). Staff memos from Development Services Director Nicole Boyer state explicitly the ordinance is "in response to House Bill 2844" and is "structured to avoid conflicts with state preemption." Every avenue the state preserved is being occupied: city-administered "site/location authorization," district-by-district zoning permission, parking and waste-handling rules, a separate "Food Truck Park" use-classification, and city-controlled noise enforcement. The drafts originated in P&Z workshops; P&Z is the formal recommending body.

3. **The architectural pattern matches the conference-center pattern.** An appointed commission (P&Z) drafts; council adopts; the regulated category is one in which seated officials' own businesses compete (Kelly Owens, Council Place 2, listed as "business owner"; Owens-family restaurants of public record). This is records-only, structural — Chapter 171 doctrine attaches as light context, not as accusation.
