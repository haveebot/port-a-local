# INFORMATION SENT SEPARATE BY CITY MANAGER FOR REVIEW

*Seven years and four months apart, two Port Aransas council votes used different procedural mechanisms to keep the deliberative material off the public packet. The bidder the 2019 rubric had winning is back this week — at the same parcel, through a different mechanism.*

---

## The lede

On the 413-page agenda packet for the **January 17, 2019** Port Aransas City Council meeting, the most consequential decision of the night fits in seven words. They appear at page 71, in the commentary line for Item 7-H — the contract awarding the city's hotel-and-conference center procurement:

> *INFORMATION SENT SEPARATE BY CITY MANAGER FOR REVIEW*

That is the entire substantive content, in the public meeting packet, of the deliberation that resulted in a 7-0 council vote awarding the project. The grading rubric the City has since confirmed was used to evaluate the two bidders is not in the packet. A full-text search of the 413 pages returns zero hits for the words *"rubric," "175," "182," "71.5,"* or *"74%."* The resolution template in the packet lists the wrong opponent — McCombs Properties rather than Palmilla Beach — and leaves the awardee field blank.

A full-text search of the eleven publicly available 2019 Port Aransas council packets — approximately 2,800 pages combined — returns the verbatim phrase *"INFORMATION SENT SEPARATE BY CITY MANAGER FOR REVIEW"* **exactly once.** Not on a routine consent item. Not on a budget line. Not on a personnel matter. On the hotel-and-conference center contract award.

The grading rubric the City has since produced under a Texas Public Information Act request shows that the bidder receiving the 7-0 votes — Cinnamon Shore, with ZJZ Hospitality as hospitality partner — scored **175 of 245 possible points (71.5 percent).** The competing bidder — Palmilla Beach — scored **182 of 245 (74 percent).** The category the lower-scoring bidder won most decisively was *"minimal impact of future tax revenue to the City."*

**On April 21, 2026 — seven years and four months later — the same City Council voted 5-2** to enter a Memorandum of Understanding and a Professional Services Agreement with KM Beach, LLC for a Hotel and Convention Center at Palmilla Beach Resort & Golf Community. The packet for that meeting carried no posted action item for the agreement. The substantive deliberation occurred in closed executive session — under exceptions for real-property and economic-development negotiations. The agenda's open-session line said only that the Council *"may take action regarding the matters which are the subjects of the above agenda item '5. Closed Executive Session.'"* The vote was reported afterward by the *Port Aransas South Jetty.*

KM Beach, LLC is a Texas-registered company headquartered at the same San Antonio address as McCombs Properties. Its filed officers are **Marsha Shields**, **Harry Ben Adams IV**, and **Steve L. Cummings.** Its assumed-name filings with the State of Texas trade as *"Palmilla Beach Golf Club"* and *"Black Marlin Bar & Grill."* KM Beach has been the named applicant on Palmilla Beach PUD replats filed with the City since at least 2022. Its parent, McCombs Properties, describes Palmilla Beach Resort on its own marketing site as *"owned and being built by McCombs Properties, the real estate development company owned by famed San Antonio businessman Red McCombs."*

This Wednesday — **May 20, 2026, 5:00 PM** — the City Council convenes again. On the public agenda, this time, are two itemized actions: a resolution *"Conditionally Consenting to the Creation of a Municipal Management District… with KM Beach, LLC"*, and the authorization of a *"temporary working group consisting of three City Council members"* to review the Master Development Agreement, for up to 90 days, with the City Manager empowered to appoint staff and legal counsel to support the committee. The deliberation of the District's geographic boundaries and the MDA's financial terms occurs, again, in closed executive session. The staff memos behind both items are listed in the agenda but not downloadable from the City's public-facing website; they are gated behind a portal account login.

The seven words on page 71 in 2019 described a decision that wasn't, in any documentary sense, made in the public meeting. The closed-session reference on April 21, 2026 described a decision that wasn't, in any documentary sense, made on the public agenda. Different mechanisms. Same outcome. Same procurement. Same parcel's family.

What the records — when assembled across sources — tell us about how Port Aransas decides is what the rest of this piece is about.

---

## What the records say

Port Aransas is governed by five named bodies that the City's organizational documents present as independent of each other:

- **City Council** — seven elected members; primary legislative + procurement-vote authority
- **Planning & Zoning Commission** — seven members appointed by Council; recommends zoning + development approvals
- **Charter Review Commission** — five-member volunteer board, statutorily six-month terms; recommends amendments to the city charter
- **Tourism Bureau Board of Directors** — sixteen voting members + ex-officio; allocates the city's Hotel Occupancy Tax pass-through, which totaled approximately $10.5 million in 2023
- **Port of Corpus Christi Authority** — regional commission with jurisdiction over Harbor Island and the ship channel adjacent to Port Aransas

The City's organizational chart is not wrong. These bodies are technically separate. They have distinct appointment processes, distinct meeting cycles, and distinct statutory jurisdictions.

In practice, looked at through the records that span all five, they are not separate. And one of the five — the Charter Review Commission — is where that fact is most starkly recorded.

---

## The Charter Review Commission

The Charter Review Commission is, by Port Aransas city statute, the body that recommends amendments to the city's charter — the document that defines the structure of the city's government, the powers and limitations of its officials, and the procedures by which any of those structures can be changed. Statutorily, it is a **five-member volunteer board appointed for six-month terms.** The Council is required to appoint five citizens of the city at its first regular meeting in July of every fourth year. The six-month term length is a design choice: the body's job is to read, recommend, and disband; new members come back when the next regular cycle is convened.

According to the Commission listing produced by the City Secretary, **Francisca Nixon**, in May 2026 in response to a Texas Public Information Act request, **three of the Commission's five seats have been held by the same residents continuously since October 15, 2020.** One seat is currently vacant. The fourth current member was appointed October 16, 2025. Of the four named members, three have completed Texas Open Meetings Act training; one has not.

Five-plus years on a six-month seat.

The same listing, in the column the City Secretary's office labels "Term Expires," names the date **April 15, 2026** for all four current members. As of the writing of this piece in May 2026, that date has passed. No City Council action publicly seating new members or formally extending the existing terms has been recorded since. The body's term of record is, at the time of writing, lapsed.

The body that drafts the rules under which Port Aransas governs itself has, by tenure and by listing, been operating outside its own statutory rotation for more than five years. Its term-of-record has been lapsed for several weeks. The next opportunity to change anything in the charter — including the rules under which the Charter Review Commission itself is appointed — is gated by the same body whose continuing tenure the rules were never designed to permit.

This is, in records terms, the structural anchor of how Port Aransas governs.

## The bodies that decide

The pattern visible in the Charter Review Commission — the body designed for rotation, in practice locked — is not unique to Charter Review. It runs across the other bodies as well.

This piece does not name individual elected officials or volunteer commissioners. The records are public — State of Texas business filings, Nueces County appraisal records, City rosters, the *Port Aransas South Jetty* archive. Readers who want to identify the individuals can. The architecture is what the piece is about. The architecture is visible without the names.

Across the four bodies — Charter Review Commission, Planning & Zoning Commission, Tourism Bureau Board, and City Council — at least **eight named households** have a principal, spouse, or sibling holding a current seat. The same households operate, collectively:

- Two of the island's largest residential real-estate brokerages
- A custom-build company with 137 permitted projects under one operating entity
- A vacation-rental management business that oversees properties on more than 700 condominium units
- A multi-restaurant operation comprising four named establishments
- A demolished-community-institution-to-private-condominium development on a single deed
- Two custom-home builders published on Palmilla Beach Resort's own marketing-site list of "Approved Expert Builders"

That last item is the cleanest cross-walk to the procurement on this Wednesday's agenda. Palmilla Beach Resort publishes seven approved custom-home builders on its developer site. **Two of the seven are operated by — or share a household with — a current city body seat-holder.** Two more of the seven are Austin-area firms with no Port Aransas governance role. Three remaining are local builders with no current city-body tie surfaced in the public record. **Two of seven, or two of the five local builders the list names**, are inside the same household network as the bodies that will, on May 20, vote on whether to consent to the Municipal Management District that finances the development the same list markets to.

A single *Port Aransas South Jetty* market roundup from July 2, 2009 names four of the principals described above — three on the residential-development side, one as a broker — as operators of adjacent projects on the same Port Aransas corridor. The architecture this piece describes is at least seventeen years old in the news-of-record.

The pattern is consistent across the bodies. Multiple seats are held by individuals whose spouses operate in the construction, brokerage, or development tier the bodies' decisions affect. Multiple seats are held by principals of two of Port Aransas's largest residential-real-estate brokerages. Multiple seats are held by employees or principals of vacation-rental operations managing hundreds of units in the city. One seat is held by an operator whose primary residence is in Corpus Christi, not Port Aransas.

This is not a finding about any individual's compliance with anything. It is a description of the seat-by-seat economic structure of the bodies that decide.

## The Tourism Bureau

The body whose composition the previous section's pattern most heavily concentrates is the same body that allocates the city's $10.5 million Hotel Occupancy Tax pass-through. The Bureau's published roster names sixteen voting members and ex-officio seats.

The current Chair is a real estate broker who previously served six years as Mayor and ten years as a City Council member, and who sat on the 2019 hotel-and-conference center grading committee whose rubric did not appear in the public meeting packet. He co-owns a Texas-registered LLC formed in May 2016 — within months of his terming out as Mayor — with two other named members. One of those co-members served as Tourism Bureau Chair preceding the current Chair's tenure and personally led the search committee that selected the Bureau's most recent prior CEO in January 2017.

Two consecutive Tourism Bureau Chairs have therefore been members of the same Texas LLC. The Chair preceding both selected the CEO who served between them. All three are real estate brokers operating on the same island.

The Bureau Board also seats — by role, on its own published roster:

- The General Manager of Vacation Rentals at the development that won the 2019 conference-center procurement
- A principal of the development that lost the 2019 procurement and is the applicant on the May 20, 2026 MMD consent vote
- An employee of a household whose other members hold seats on Council and the Planning & Zoning Commission
- A representative of one of the national corporate short-term-rental management platforms now operating at scale in Port Aransas — Sway, since acquired by Avantstay
- The city's career City Manager, **David Parsons**, ex-officio

The career City Manager has held the post across multiple Council compositions, sat on the 2019 grading committee, and remains the executor of HOT-funded contracts the Bureau directs.

The Bureau is, on its public roster, the social ledger of how Port Aransas decides. It does not need to be opaque. It is published.

## What the disclosure forms cannot see

This piece would, in another city, have at this point reported on the conflict-of-interest filings that Texas Local Government Code Chapters 171 and 176 require local government officers and the vendors they transact with to maintain.

Port A Local filed a Texas Public Information Act request asking the City of Port Aransas whether any **Form CIQ** — the vendor disclosure required when a vendor's relationship with a sitting officer crosses statutory thresholds — was on file from 2017 through 2026 naming Sea Oats Group LLC, ZJZ Hospitality LLC, Cinnamon Shore (or its General Partner), or any of their named principals.

The City Secretary's response, in writing: *"The City of Port Aransas has identified no records responsive to this portion of your request. This portion of your request is now closed."*

Zero records. Nine years. The Form CIQ regime is a vendor-side disclosure with a § 176.006 trigger that may not have fired in any of the relationships above. The form's design captures vendor relationships with named officers; the form does not capture the kind of household-network overlap this piece describes. The officer-side equivalent — Form CIS filings under § 176.003, and Chapter 171.004 substantial-interest abstention affidavits — is the subject of a focused follow-up records request now pending with the City Secretary.

**A second Texas Public Information Act request will be filed after the May 20, 2026 vote.** That request will ask the City Secretary whether any conflict-of-interest filing naming **KM Beach, LLC, Palmilla Beach Resort, McCombs Properties, Marsha Shields, Harry Ben Adams IV, or Steve L. Cummings** is on file with the City from 2017 through 2026. It will also ask the City Secretary to identify every agenda item in that nine-year period in which deliberative materials were sent to Council outside the publicly distributed agenda packet — by any mechanism — and to provide the records that document those routings. The response will be published in a follow-up Dispatch.

The architecture the records describe is not, in itself, a disclosure-form violation. It is a description of an architecture the forms were not designed to surface.

## What the public packet does not contain

Port A Local has attempted to retrieve, from the City's public-facing channels, the staff memo for the May 20, 2026 Resolution conditionally consenting to the Municipal Management District (AIR-26-067), the staff memo authorizing the three-member Master Development Agreement working group (AIR-26-078), and the minutes of the April 21, 2026 Council meeting at which the Memorandum of Understanding and Professional Services Agreement with KM Beach, LLC was approved.

The City lists all three documents on its civicweb agenda portal, but each requires a user account login to download. The City's public-facing website at cityofportaransas.org does not currently host the same documents. The 2019 hotel-and-conference center procurement packet — the one that contains the seven words on page 71 — is, by comparison, freely downloadable from the City's public website at *cityofportaransas.org/wp-content/uploads/2020/01/Agenda-Packet-1-17-19.pdf.*

The newer documents do not appear there.

The deliberative material the public packet of January 17, 2019 routed outside the packet was the rubric that produced the 7-0 vote. The deliberative material the public agenda of April 21, 2026 routed outside the agenda was the MOU and PSA that produced the 5-2 vote. The deliberative material the public agenda of May 20, 2026 will route outside the publicly retrievable record, if the access pattern holds, will be the MMD's geographic boundaries, the MDA's financial terms, and the staff memos that explain both.

Three procurement-cycle decisions, three different procedural mechanisms, the same outcome regarding the public's access to the deliberative record.

## The pattern, in motion

The architecture is not dormant. It is currently producing decisions.

In 2025 the State of Texas passed a law that, beginning July 1, 2026, preempts local food-truck health-and-sanitation permitting, caps the state license at $150 plus inspection, and leaves municipalities authority only over zoning, location, parking, fire, and noise.

On April 21, 2026 — the same Council meeting that closed its executive session on the Palmilla agreement — the City Council voted approval of first readings on three ordinance drafts that occupy every avenue the state did not preempt: a "Mobile Food Vending" rule, a zoning amendment, and a noise amendment. On May 20, 2026 the same three ordinances return for **second readings.** The drafts include a new annual city site/location authorization, district-by-district zoning gates, a multi-vendor "Food Truck Park" classification, parking and waste and restroom standards, and noise enforcement. The drafts were prepared in Planning & Zoning workshops and are formally recommended by P&Z to Council. The City's Development Services Director, in the public meeting packet, describes the ordinance as *"structured to avoid conflicts with state preemption."*

The state's law takes effect July 1, 2026. The city is moving to lock its gate before that date arrives.

The pattern — Planning & Zoning prepares, Council adopts, the bodies that decide preserve the maximum scope the state has not yet taken — is the same pattern the rest of this piece has described. The conference-center procurement was a single large decision that flowed through it. The food-truck ordinance is a small one. The architecture is the same.

## What gets built when this is who decides

The 2019 Hotel and Conference Center procurement awarded a 7-0 vote to a project located inside Cinnamon Shore II. Two of the seven who voted on that contract had personally helped develop and apply the grading rubric whose contents the public packet did not contain. The other five had not seen the rubric in any publicly distributed materials. Both members who served on the grading committee retained their Council seats through 2026. Both voted in favor of the April 21, 2026 Memorandum of Understanding with KM Beach, LLC.

Public meeting records confirm that on July 16, 2020 — eighteen months after the 2019 award — the same City Council voted 7-0 to relocate the project from Cinnamon Shore II to the Brookdale parcel two miles north on State Highway 361, under a different ownership entity (*"PA Waterfront, LP / The Brookdale Group, LLC"*). On November 19, 2020, the Council passed Resolution 2020-R59 extending the Development Agreement with a third entity, *"The Inn, Spa & Conference Center, LP,"* through December 31, 2022. The developer's filed timeline projected closing on land February 2021, PUD amendment August 2021, infrastructure design December 2021, and groundbreaking December 2022.

As of May 2026, no hotel-and-conference center has been constructed on the parcel originally awarded the contract or on the Brookdale parcel.

The procurement cycle did not, however, end. On April 21, 2026 the Council entered the agreement with **KM Beach, LLC** — the McCombs Properties operating entity that owns the parcel of the 2019 procurement's higher-scoring bidder. The reported project cost is approximately **$66 million.** The financing is to be structured as a Tax Increment Reinvestment Zone and a Municipal Management District — two mechanisms by which public revenue is captured to fund private development on a particular parcel. The location is **Beach Access Road 1, within Palmilla Beach Resort and Golf Community.** On May 20, 2026, the same Council is scheduled to vote on the resolution conditionally consenting to the District, and to authorize a three-member Council working group, for up to 90 days, to negotiate the Master Development Agreement.

The pattern of demolition-then-development-without-completion is not isolated to the 2019 conference-center procurement. In July 2022, Fisherman's Wharf Holding LLC — operated by a current Tourism Bureau Board member whose primary residence is in Corpus Christi — demolished a longtime Port Aransas community institution at 136 W Cotter to construct The Aransas Club, a 26-unit private condominium development. As of mid-2025, the project's marketing language described its inventory as *"last remaining harborfront properties"* — that is, still selling. Two prominent post-Harvey Port Aransas real-estate-development decisions produced, between them, one demolished community asset and one slow-selling private condominium tower, and zero completed hotel-and-conference centers.

The next attempt is scheduled for this Wednesday.

The City Manager who signed the 2019 grading committee — **David Parsons** — remains the City Manager today. He recommended the March 2025 contract awarded 7-0 to Weaver and Jacobs Constructors, for $9.575 million, to expand the City Hall and Civic Center — with Turner Ramirez Architects as the project's architect, the same firm that was on Cinnamon Shore's 2019 winning conference-center bid team. He recommended the April 21, 2026 agreement with KM Beach, LLC. He is the official the May 20 working-group resolution authorizes to appoint staff and legal counsel to support the committee's MDA negotiations.

A full-text search of the eleven publicly available 2019 council packets returns the phrase *"INFORMATION SENT SEPARATE BY CITY MANAGER FOR REVIEW"* exactly once — on the hotel-and-conference center contract item. Not on a routine consent matter. Not on a budget. Not on a personnel item. The publicly retrievable record after 2019 sits behind a portal login and cannot be searched the same way; the records request filed after the May 20 vote will ask the City Secretary for that count directly.

## The fix path is the same body

The Charter Review Commission described earlier in this piece — three of five seats held by the same residents since October 2020, one vacant, one without current Texas Open Meetings Act training, all four named members carrying a *"Term Expires"* date of April 15, 2026 that has now passed — is the body the city's charter requires to be seated to recommend any amendment to that charter. The next regular cycle for the Commission's full reseating, by city statute, is July 2029, unless an extraordinary act of the same Council whose composition the records describe seats one earlier.

The architecture is, in this sense, internally consistent. The body that would produce a recommendation to change the architecture is the body whose composition the architecture has produced. The body's terms have lapsed; the body continues. Its members have been read and recommended together since the procurement cycle that produced the seven words on page 71.

## Close

Leadership decides what happens to a town — not culture or destiny.

The decisions the records describe are decisions, not weather. The bodies that produced them are documented. The structural composition of those bodies is documented. The pattern across the bodies, when assembled from records that exist and have always existed, is documented.

The next set of decisions is on **this Wednesday's agenda.** A resolution conditionally consenting to a Municipal Management District. A three-member working group, ninety days, with authority to negotiate the Master Development Agreement framework. The deliberation will, again, occur in closed executive session.

Port A Local will file two records requests after the May 20 vote. The first will ask the City Secretary whether any conflict-of-interest filing naming KM Beach, LLC, Palmilla Beach Resort, McCombs Properties, Marsha Shields, Harry Ben Adams IV, or Steve L. Cummings is on file with the City from 2017 through 2026. The second will ask the City Secretary to identify every agenda item in that nine-year period in which deliberative materials were sent to Council outside the publicly distributed agenda packet — by any mechanism — and to provide the records that document those routings. The responses will be published in a follow-up Dispatch.

The records describe an architecture. The architecture was decided. The next set of decisions is still open.
